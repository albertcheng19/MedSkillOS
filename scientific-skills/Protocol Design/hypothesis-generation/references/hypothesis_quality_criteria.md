# Hypothesis Quality Criteria

## Framework for Evaluating Scientific Hypotheses

Use these criteria to evaluate the quality and rigor of generated hypotheses. A robust hypothesis should perform well across multiple dimensions.

**Note on Report Structure:** When generating hypothesis reports, please provide a brief quality assessment summary (with a comparative table of scores) in the main body, and include a detailed assessment, strengths, weaknesses, and comprehensive analysis in **Appendix C: Quality Assessment**.

## Core Criteria

### 1. Testability

**Definition:** The hypothesis can be empirically tested through observation or experimentation.

**Evaluation Questions:**
- Can the hypothesis be tested through specific experiments or observations?
- Are the predicted outcomes measurable?
- Can the hypothesis be tested using current or near-future methods?
- Are there multiple independent ways to test it?

**Strong Testability Examples:**
- "Increased expression of Protein X will decrease cell proliferation rate by >30%"
- "Patients receiving Treatment Y will have a 50% reduction in Symptom Z within 4 weeks"

**Weak Testability Examples:**
- "This process is influenced by complex interactions" (vague, no specific prediction)
- "The mechanism involves quantum effects" (if no method to test quantum effects exists)

### 2. Falsifiability

**Definition:** Clear conditions or observations exist that could prove the hypothesis wrong (Popperian criterion).

**Evaluation Questions:**
- What specific observations would prove the hypothesis false?
- Are the falsification conditions realistic and feasible?
- Is the hypothesis stated clearly enough to be falsified?
- Can null results effectively falsify the hypothesis?

**Strong Falsifiability Examples:**
- "If we knock out Gene X, Phenotype Y will disappear" (falsifiable if the phenotype persists)
- "Drug A outperforms placebo in 80% of patients" (clear falsification threshold)

**Weak Falsifiability Examples:**
- "Multiple factors contribute to the outcome" (too vague to falsify)
- "Effects may vary depending on context" (built-in escape clauses)

### 3. Parsimony (Occam's Razor)

**Definition:** Among competing hypotheses with equal explanatory power, the simpler explanation is preferred.

**Evaluation Questions:**
- Does the hypothesis invoke the minimum number of entities/mechanisms required for the explanation?
- Are all proposed elements necessary to explain the phenomenon?
- Is there a simpler mechanism that could explain the observations?
- Does it avoid unnecessary assumptions?

**Parsimony Considerations:**
- Simple ≠ simplistic; complexity is justified when evidence requires it.
- Established mechanisms are "simpler" than novel, unproven ones.
- Direct mechanisms are simpler than complex multi-step pathways.
- One well-supported mechanism is preferred over multiple speculative ones.

### 4. Explanatory Power

**Definition:** The hypothesis is able to explain a significant portion of the observed phenomena.

**Evaluation Questions:**
- How much of the observed data does the hypothesis explain?
- Does it explain both typical and atypical observations?
- Can it explain related phenomena beyond the immediate observation?
- Does it resolve apparent contradictions in existing data?

**Strong Explanatory Power Indicators:**
- Explains multiple independent observations.
- Explains quantitative relationships, not just qualitative patterns.
- Resolves previously puzzling findings.
- Makes seemingly contradictory results plausible.

**Limited Explanatory Power Indicators:**
- Explains only a fraction of the phenomenon.
- Requires additional hypotheses to complete the explanation.
- Leaves major observations unexplained.

### 5. Scope

**Definition:** The range of phenomena and contexts the hypothesis can cover.

**Evaluation Questions:**
- Does it apply only to a specific case or to a broader set of circumstances?
- Can it be generalized across conditions, species, or systems?
- Does it link to a larger theoretical framework?
- What are its boundaries and limitations?

**Broader Scope (usually preferred):**
- Applies to multiple experimental conditions.
- Generalizable to related systems or species.
- Links the phenomenon to established principles.

**Narrower Scope (acceptable if clearly defined):**
- Limited to specific conditions or contexts.
- Requires different mechanisms in different environments.
- Context-dependency with clear boundaries.

### 6. Consistency with Established Knowledge

**Definition:** Consistency with well-supported theories, principles, and empirical findings.

**Evaluation Questions:**
- Is it consistent with established physical, chemical, or biological principles?
- Does it fit within or reasonably extend current theory?
- If it contradicts established knowledge, is there a strong justification?
- Does it require violating well-supported laws or findings?

**Consistency Levels:**
- **Fully Consistent:** Applying established mechanisms in a new context.
- **Largely Consistent:** Extends current understanding in a plausible way.
- **Partially Inconsistent:** Contradicts some findings but has explanatory value.
- **Highly Inconsistent:** Requires rejecting accepted principles (requires extraordinary evidence).

### 7. Novelty and Insight

**Definition:** The hypothesis provides new understanding beyond merely restating known facts.

**Evaluation Questions:**
- Does it provide new mechanistic insights?
- Does it challenge assumptions or conventional wisdom?
- Does it suggest unexpected connections or relationships?
- Does it open new directions for research?

**Novelty Contributions:**
- Proposes a mechanism not previously considered.
- Reframes the problem in a productive way.
- Connects unrelated observations.
- Suggests non-obvious testable predictions.

**Note:** Novelty alone does not make a hypothesis value; it must also possess testability, parsimony, and explanatory power.

## Comparative Evaluation

When evaluating multiple competing hypotheses:

### Trade-offs and Balancing

Hypotheses often involve trade-offs:
- More parsimonious but with less explanatory power.
- Broader scope but harder to test with current methods.
- Novel insights but lower consistency with current knowledge.

**Evaluation Method:**
- No hypothesis needs to be perfect in all dimensions.
- Identify strengths and weaknesses of each hypothesis.
- Consider which criteria are most important for the specific phenomenon.
- Flag which hypotheses are most immediately testable.
- Identify which hypotheses would provide the most information if supported.

### Distinguishability

**Key Question:** Can experiments distinguish between competing hypotheses?

- Identify predictions where the hypotheses differ.
- Prioritize hypotheses that make unique predictions.
- Flag which experiments would most effectively narrow the field.
- Consider if hypotheses might all be partially correct.

## Common Pitfalls

### Untestable Hypotheses
- Too vague to generate specific predictions.
- Invokes unobservable or unmeasurable entities.
- Requires technology that does not yet exist.

### Unfalsifiable Hypotheses
- Built-in escape clauses ("may or may not occur").
- Post-hoc explanations that can accommodate any outcome.
- Fails to specify what would prove it wrong.

### Overly Complex Hypotheses
- Invokes multiple unproven mechanisms.
- Adds unnecessary steps or entities.
- Complexity is not justified by an increase in explanatory power.

### Just-So Stories
- Plausible narratives without testable predictions.
- Explains observations but fails to predict new ones.
- Indistinguishable from alternative explanations.

## Practical Application

When generating hypotheses:

1. **Draft initial hypotheses**, focusing on mechanistic explanations.
2. **Apply quality criteria** to identify weaknesses.
3. **Refine hypotheses** to improve testability and clarity.
4. **Develop specific predictions** to enhance testability and falsifiability.
5. **Systematically compare** across all criteria.
6. **Prioritize testing** based on distinguishability and feasibility.

Remember: The goal is not a perfect hypothesis, but a set of testable, falsifiable, and informative hypotheses that advance the understanding of a phenomenon.
