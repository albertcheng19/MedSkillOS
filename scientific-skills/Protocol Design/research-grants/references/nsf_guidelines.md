# NSF (National Science Foundation) Proposal Writing Guide

## Agency Overview

**Mission**: To promote the progress of science; to advance the national health, prosperity, and welfare; and to secure the national defense.

**Annual Budget**: Approximately $9–10 billion.

**Website**: https://www.nsf.gov

**Core Characteristics**:
- Supports all fields of fundamental science and engineering (except medical sciences).
- Emphasizes education and workforce development.
- Strong commitment to diversity, equity, and inclusion (DEI).
- Promotes open science and data sharing.
- Encourages collaborative research across institutions.

## NSF Directorates

1. **BIO** - Biological Sciences
2. **CISE** - Computer and Information Science and Engineering
3. **EDU** - STEM Education (formerly EHR - Education and Human Resources)
4. **ENG** - Engineering
5. **GEO** - Geosciences
6. **MPS** - Mathematical and Physical Sciences

8. **TIP** - Technology, Innovation and Partnerships (formerly EDA)
9. **OPP** - Office of Polar Programs
10. **OISE** - Office of International Science and Engineering

## Core Review Criteria

NSF uses two merit review criteria, which are given equal weight:

### Intellectual Merit

**Definition**: The potential to advance knowledge.

**Evaluation Questions**:
- How important is the proposed activity to advancing knowledge and understanding within its own field or across different fields?
- To what extent do the proposed activities suggest and explore creative, original, or potentially transformative concepts?
- Is the plan for carrying out the proposed activities well-reasoned, well-organized, and based on a sound rationale?
- How well qualified is the individual, team, or organization to conduct the proposed activities?
- Are there adequate resources available to the PI (either at the home organization or through collaborations) to carry out the proposed activities?

**Writing Strategies**:
- Open with the research question and its significance.
- Demonstrate deep knowledge of the field.
- Clearly articulate the knowledge gap.
- Propose an innovative approach to address the gap.
- Present preliminary results or proof of concept.
- Demonstrate team qualifications.
- Present a feasible and well-organized plan.

### Broader Impacts

**Definition**: The potential to benefit society and contribute to the achievement of specific, desired societal outcomes.

**Evaluation Questions**:
- What is the potential for the proposed activity to:
  - Benefit society or advance desired societal outcomes?
  - Broaden the participation of underrepresented groups?
  - Enhance the infrastructure for research and education?
  - Increase public scientific literacy and public engagement with science and technology?
  - Foster partnerships between academia, industry, and others?

**Key Point**: Broader Impacts are never an afterthought. They carry equal weight with Intellectual Merit and must be substantive, specific, and measurable.

**The Five Pillars of Broader Impacts** (address at least one substantively):

1. **Advance Discovery and Understanding while Promoting Teaching, Training, and Learning**
   - Integrate research into the curriculum.
   - Develop new instructional materials.
   - Train undergraduate students, graduate students, and postdoctoral researchers.
   - Provide research experiences for students.
   - Create educational resources (videos, software, databases).
   - Host workshops or training programs.

   *Example*: "We will develop a 10-module online course in computational genomics incorporating data from this project, accessible to over 500 students annually across 15 partner institutions. Course materials will be open-access and include Jupyter notebooks for hands-on analysis."

2. **Broaden Participation of Underrepresented Groups (in STEM)**
   - Partner with Minority Serving Institutions (HBCUs, HSIs, TCUs).
   - Recruit students from underrepresented groups.
   - Provide mentoring and support programs.
   - Address systemic barriers to participation.
   - Foster an inclusive research environment.
   - Engage underrepresented communities in research.

   *Example*: "We will establish a summer research program for 8 undergraduate students annually from 4 partner HBCUs, providing stipends, housing, and year-round mentoring. The program includes career development workshops and pathways to graduate school."

3. **Enhance Infrastructure for Research and Education**
   - Develop shared instrumentation or facilities.
   - Create cyberinfrastructure, software, or databases.
   - Establish collaborative networks.
   - Build living resource centers or repositories.
   - Develop standards or protocols.
   - Create open-source tools.

   *Example*: "We will develop and maintain an open-source software platform for analyzing spatial transcriptomics data, complete with extensive documentation, tutorials, and a user support forum. The software will be hosted on GitHub and indexed in bio.tools."

4. **Dissemination to Enhance Scientific and Technological Understanding**
   - Public outreach and science communication.
   - Engagement with K-12 students and teachers.
   - Museum exhibits or science festivals.
   - Media engagement (podcasts, videos, articles).
   - Policy briefs for decision-makers.
   - Community science projects.

   *Example*: "We will partner with the City Science Museum to create an interactive exhibit on AI and climate modeling, expected to reach over 50,000 visitors annually. The exhibit will include interactive simulations and bilingual materials. We will also host quarterly 'Science Saturdays' for local K-12 students."

5. **Benefits to Society**
   - Economic development and competitiveness.
   - Improvements in health and quality of life.
   - Environmental sustainability.
   - National security.
   - Societal well-being.
   - Workforce development.

   *Example*: "Our drought prediction models will be integrated into the USDA decision support system, benefiting over 15,000 farmers in the Southwest. We will work with extension agents to provide training and a user-friendly interface for non-technical users."

**Common Broader Impacts Mistakes**:
- ❌ Vague statements: "We will train graduate students" (everyone does this).
- ❌ Lack of a plan: Grand goals without specific activities.
- ❌ No metrics: No way to evaluate success.
- ❌ "Bolt-on" approach: Not integrated with the research plan.
- ❌ Unrealistic: Making grand claims without resources or expertise.
- ✅ Specific and Measurable: Clear activities, timelines, and evaluation.

## Proposal Sections and Page Limits

### Project Summary (1 page)

**Required Structure** (NSF mandates three labeled sections):

**Overview** (First paragraph):
- Introduce the research problem and approach in lay language.
- Suitable for dissemination to the public.

**Intellectual Merit**:
- Potential to advance knowledge.
- Innovation.
- Team qualifications.

**Broader Impacts**:
- Societal benefits and specific activities.
- How success will be measured.

**Formatting**: Must strictly use the section headings above.

### Project Description (15 pages for most programs)

**No mandated structure, but typical organization is as follows**:

1. **Introduction / Background** (1-2 pages)
   - Research problem and significance.
   - State of current knowledge.
   - Knowledge gaps.
   - Preliminary results (if applicable).

2. **Research Objectives** (0.5-1 page)
   - Specific, measurable goals.
   - Hypotheses or research questions.

3. **Research Plan / Methodology** (8-10 pages)
   - Detailed approach for each objective.
   - Methods and techniques.
   - Timeline and milestones.
   - Expected outcomes.
   - Potential challenges and alternatives.

4. **Broader Impacts** (1-2 pages)
   - Can be integrated throughout or as a standalone section.
   - Specific activities and timeline.
   - Assessment and evaluation plan.

5. **Results from Prior NSF Support** (if applicable, up to 5 pages)
   - Required if the PI or co-PI has received an NSF award in the last 5 years.
   - Intellectual merit of prior work.
   - Broader impacts of prior work.
   - Publications and outcomes.

**Formatting Requirements**:
- Font: 11 point or larger (Times Roman, Arial, Palatino, Computer Modern).
- Margins: 1 inch on all sides.
- Line spacing: No more than 6 lines per inch.
- Paper size: 8.5 x 11 inches.
- No smaller fonts in figures (must be legible).

### References Cited (No page limit)

- Each reference must include:
  - All author names.
  - Article and journal title.
  - Volume, page numbers, year.
  - DOI (if available).
- Use a consistent format (does not have to match a specific style).
- Information must be sufficient for a reviewer to find the reference.

### Biographical Sketch (Up to 3 pages per person)

**NSF Required Format** (per 2023 PAPPG):

**Section A: Professional Preparation**
- Undergraduate, graduate, and postdoctoral institutions.
- Major and degree, including year.

**Section B: Appointments and Positions**
- 5 most recent positions, starting with the current position.

**Section C: Products** (Up to 5 products most relevant to the proposal)
- Publications, datasets, software, patents, etc.
- Can include products in preparation.

**Section D: Synergistic Activities** (Up to 5 items)
- Service, teaching, mentoring, outreach.
- Demonstrate broad engagement beyond research.

### Current and Pending Support (No page limit)

- All current and pending support for PI and co-PIs.
- Include project/proposal title, source, amount, and dates.
- Describe overlap with the proposed project (if any).
- Must be updated continuously until award/rejection.

### Facilities, Equipment, and Other Resources (No page limit)

- Describe available facilities (labs, computing, libraries).
- Major equipment available for the project.
- Other resources (personnel, core facilities, partnerships).
- Demonstrate institutional commitment to support.

### Data Management and Sharing Plan (Up to 2 pages)

**Required for all proposals** (per 2023 PAPPG)

**Must cover**:
1. **Data Types**: What data will be generated?
2. **Standards**: Formats for data and metadata, metadata, standards.
3. **Access**: How and when will data be shared?
4. **Reuse**: Who can access and under what conditions?
5. **Repositories**: Where will data be archived long-term?
6. **Protection**: Privacy, confidentiality, intellectual property considerations.

**NSF Expectations**:
- Data should be made available to the public in a timely manner.
- Use discipline-specific repositories whenever possible.
- Justify any restrictions on data sharing.
- Plan for data preservation beyond the project period.

### Postdoctoral Researcher Mentoring Plan (Up to 1 page)

**Required if the budget includes funding for a postdoc**

**Must cover**:
- Career development goals.
- Mentoring activities (research, teaching, professional skills).
- Success metrics.
- Mentoring plan should be specific, not generic.

## Special NSF Proposal Types

### CAREER (Faculty Early Career Development Program)

**Eligibility**: Tenure-track (or equivalent) faculty who have not yet received tenure, within 6 years of receiving their PhD (or equivalent).

**Requirements**:
- Integration of research and education.
- Demonstrate leadership potential.
- Departmental letter required.
- 5-year project plan.
- Typical budget: $400,000–$500,000.

**Key Elements**:
- Ambitious research plan.
- Innovative education component.
- Strong integration (not just parallel tracks).
- Pathway to independence and leadership.
- Institutional commitment.

**Review Criteria**: Same two criteria (Intellectual Merit, Broader Impacts), but emphasizing:
- Integration of research and education.
- Innovative education component.
- Leadership potential in the field.

**Common CAREER Mistakes**:
- Education component feels "tacked on."
- Research plan is over-ambitious.
- Weak link between research and education.
- Generic mentoring or teaching plans.
- Insufficient preliminary data.

### Collaborative Research

**Structure**: Multiple proposals submitted separately by different institutions, reviewed as a single project.

**Requirements**:
- Designate a lead institution.
- All proposals must have the same title (except for institution name).
- Project descriptions should be essentially identical.
- Clear division of labor.
- Coordination plan.

**Budget**: Each institution submits its own budget for its portion.

**Review**: Reviewed together as a complete project.

**Benefit**: Brings together complementary expertise and resources.

### RAPID (Rapid Response Research)

**Purpose**: To support research with a severe urgency of timing.

**Examples**:
- Natural disasters.
- Disease outbreaks.
- Unique astronomical events.
- Rare data collection opportunities.

**Requirements**:
- Justification of urgent need.
- Up to $200,000.
- Up to 1-year duration.
- Streamlined review (decided by Program Officer).
- No preliminary data required.

**Submission**: Contact the Program Officer first, then submit the proposal.

### EAGER (Early-concept Grants for Exploratory Research)

**Purpose**: To support exploratory work on untested but potentially transformative ideas.

**Requirements**:
- High-risk, high-reward research.
- Radically different approaches.
- Up to $300,000.
- Up to 2 years.
- Requires Program Officer approval before submission.
- No panel review (decided by Program Officer).

**Key**: Must be truly exploratory and high-risk, not incremental research.

## Budgetary Considerations

### Allowable Costs

**Personnel**:
- Senior Personnel: Up to 2 months of salary (summer salary) for faculty on 9-month appointments.
- Postdoctoral Scholars: Full salary and benefits.
- Graduate Students: Stipends (tuition is usually covered under benefits/indirects).
- Undergraduate Students: Hourly wages or stipends.
- Technical and Administrative Staff.

**Fringe Benefits**: Follow institutional rates.

**Equipment**: Items with a unit cost ≥$5,000.
- Must be justified.
- Shared equipment requires letters from collaborators.

**Travel**:
- Domestic and international academic conferences.
- Collaboration and field work.
- Requires justification.

**Participant Support Costs**: For workshops, training, conferences.
- Stipends, travel, subsistence for participants.
- No indirect costs (F&A) are calculated on these.

**Other Direct Costs**:
- Publication costs.
- Consultant services.
- Computer services.
- Materials and supplies.
- Subawards to collaborating institutions.

**Indirect Costs (F&A)**: Institutionally negotiated rates applied to Modified Total Direct Costs (MTDC).
- MTDC excludes: Equipment, participant support, subawards beyond $25K.

### Cost Sharing

**NSF Policy**: Cost sharing is not required and should not be voluntary.

**Exceptions**: Certain programs require cost sharing (check the solicitation).

**If included**: Must be documented, verifiable, auditable, and necessary for the project.

## Submission and Review Process

### Submission Deadlines

**Varies by program**:
- Some programs have specific deadlines (e.g., twice a year).
- Some programs accept proposals anytime (consult the Program Officer).
- CAREER: July deadline (varies by Directorate).

**Submission Window**: NSF deadlines are typically 5:00 PM local time of the submitter's location.

### Submission Portals

**Research.gov** or **Grants.gov**: NSF accepts both.

**Process**:
1. Requires institutional authorization.
2. Upload all required documents.
3. Check PDF generation.
4. Submit (aim for 48 hours early).
5. Receive confirmation and proposal number.

### Review Process

**Timeline**: Typically 6 months from submission to decision.

**Steps**:
1. **Administrative Review**: NSF checks for compliance (1-2 weeks).
2. **Assign Program Officer**: Assigned to the appropriate program (1-2 weeks).
3. **Select Reviewers**: Identify panel and/or ad hoc reviewers (2-4 weeks).
4. **Review**: Reviewers evaluate the proposal (4-8 weeks).
5. **Panel Discussion**: Panel meeting (virtual or in-person) to discuss proposals (1 week).
6. **Program Officer Recommendation**: Based on reviews and panel discussion (2-4 weeks).
7. **Directorate/Office Approval**: Final decision (2-4 weeks).

**Review Formats**:
- **Panel Review**: 10-20 proposals discussed at a panel meeting.
- **Ad hoc Review**: External reviewers submit written comments.
- **Mixed Mode**: Combination of panel and ad hoc reviews.

**Number of Reviewers**: Typically 3-5 reviewers per proposal.

### Review Results

**Possible Decisions**:
- **Funded**: Congratulations! Award is forthcoming.
- **Declined**: Not recommended for funding.
- **Returned Without Review**: Did not meet compliance requirements.

**Feedback**: Regardless of the outcome, a panel summary and individual reviews are provided.

**Success Rate**: Varies by program, typically 15-30%.

## Communicating with Program Officers

### When to Contact

**Appropriate Situations**:
- Pre-submission: Discuss fit with the program, feasibility of the idea.
- Post-review: Discuss feedback, resubmission strategy.
- During the project: Report major changes, request no-cost extensions.

**How to Contact**:
- Email the Program Officer (contact info in the solicitation).
- Request a 15-30 minute phone call.
- Prepare a concise summary of the research idea (1 page).

### What to Ask

**Good Questions**:
- Does my research fit this program?
- Are there any recent solicitations or special initiatives?
- What are the priority areas for this program?
- Is the scale and budget appropriate?
- Post-review: What were the key concerns to address in a resubmission?

**Avoid**:
- Asking for a guarantee of funding.
- Arguing about the review results.
- Inappropriately asking for reviewer information.

## Resubmission Strategy

### NSF Resubmission Policy

**No formal resubmission category**: NSF treats resubmitted proposals as new proposals.

**Can resubmit**:
- To the same program (after addressing reviews).
- To a different program (if a better fit).
- After making substantive changes.

**No introduction section**: Unlike NIH, NSF has no formal resubmission response section.

**Strategy**:
- Carefully read the panel summary and individual reviews.
- Respond to all major criticisms.
- Strengthen weak areas (preliminary data, broader impacts, methodology).
- Consider talking to the Program Officer.
- May need to wait for the next cycle to gather more data.

**Tracking**: Previously reviewed proposals are sometimes assigned to the same reviewers.

## Tips for Competitive NSF Proposals

### Do's

✅ **Start with specific goals/tasks** - Research objectives should be crystal clear.
✅ **Make Broader Impacts substantive** - Specific activities, not clichés.
✅ **Use figures and diagrams effectively** - Conceptual maps, preliminary data, timelines.
✅ **Set a realistic scope** - Achievable within 3-5 years.
✅ **Address both review criteria explicitly** - Don't make reviewers hunt for them.
✅ **Get external feedback** - Conduct a mock review before submission.
✅ **Follow formatting requirements strictly** - Non-compliance leads to automatic rejection.
✅ **Explain jargon and acronyms** - Panelists may not be in your specific niche.
✅ **Integrate research and education** - Show natural connections.
✅ **Demonstrate team qualifications** - Track record in the proposed area.

### Don'ts

❌ **Don't exceed page limits** - Will be returned without review.
❌ **Don't use tiny fonts in figures** - Must be legible.
❌ **Don't make Broader Impacts generic** - "Training students" is not enough.
❌ **Don't ignore prior NSF support** - Must report if you've had an NSF award.
❌ **Don't be over-ambitious** - Reviewers can see through unrealistic plans.
❌ **Don't skip the Data Management Plan** - Required for all proposals.
❌ **Don't forget biosketches for all personnel** - A common mistake.
❌ **Don't submit on the deadline day** - Technical issues can happen.
❌ **Don't ignore the solicitation** - Requirements vary by program.
❌ **Don't assume reviewers know your work** - Provide context and background.
