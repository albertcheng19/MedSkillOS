# DOE (U.S. Department of Energy) Grant Application Writing Guide

## Agency Overview

**Mission**: To ensure America's security and prosperity by addressing its energy, environmental, and nuclear challenges through transformative science and technology solutions.

**Annual Budget**: Approximately $50 billion (including National Laboratories, energy programs, and nuclear security).

**Official Website**: https://www.energy.gov

**Core Features**:
- Focuses on energy, climate, environment, computing, and physical sciences.
- Manages 17 National Laboratories (the largest scientific infrastructure in the U.S.).
- Emphasizes industry-academia-research collaboration and commercialization.
- Covers all stages from basic science to applied R&D.
- Usually requires cost sharing.
- Prioritizes national security and energy security.

## Major DOE Offices and Programs

### Office of Science (SC)

**Budget**: Approximately $8 billion (the largest supporter of physical science research in the U.S.).

**Mission**: To deliver scientific discoveries and major scientific tools to transform our understanding of nature and advance the energy, economic, and national security of the United States.

**Program Offices**:

1. **Advanced Scientific Computing Research (ASCR)**
   - High-performance computing
   - Applied mathematics
   - Computational science
   - Exascale computing

2. **Basic Energy Sciences (BES)**
   - Materials sciences and engineering
   - Chemical sciences
   - Condensed matter and materials physics
   - User facilities (light sources, neutron sources)

3. **Biological and Environmental Research (BER)**
   - Biological systems science
   - Climate and environmental sciences
   - Environmental Molecular Sciences Laboratory (EMSL)

4. **Fusion Energy Sciences (FES)**
   - Plasma physics
   - Fusion energy development
   - ITER international collaboration

5. **High Energy Physics (HEP)**
   - Particle physics
   - Accelerator science
   - Quantum Information Science (QIS)

6. **Nuclear Physics (NP)**
   - Nuclear structure and dynamics
   - Relativistic heavy ions
   - Fundamental symmetries

**Funding Mechanisms**:
- **Early Career Research Program**: Provides $750,000 over 5 years for early-career scientists.
- **Funding Opportunity Announcements (FOAs)**: Solicitations for specific programs.
- **Laboratory Directed Research and Development (LDRD)**: For National Laboratory employees.

### ARPA-E (Advanced Research Projects Agency-Energy)

**Mission**: To advance high-potential, high-impact energy technologies that are too early for private-sector investment.

**Features**:
- High-risk, high-reward transformative energy technologies.
- Requires cost sharing (typically 20% for universities, higher for industry).
- Emphasizes commercialization pathways.
- Rigorous project management and milestone assessments.
- Budget: Approximately $500 million per year.

**Program Types**:
- **Focused Programs**: Specific technology areas (announced via FOAs).
- **OPEN**: General solicitations across all energy technologies.
- **SCALEUP**: A bridge connecting the lab to the market.

**Typical Funding**:
- $1 million to $10 million per project.
- Duration of 1 to 3 years.
- Focuses on technology transition.

### Office of Energy Efficiency and Renewable Energy (EERE)

**Mission**: To accelerate the development and deployment of clean energy technologies.

**Program Areas**:
- **Solar Energy Technologies Office (SETO)**
- **Wind Energy Technologies Office (WETO)**
- **Water Power Technologies Office (WPTO)**
- **Geothermal Technologies Office (GTO)**
- **Building Technologies Office (BTO)**
- **Advanced Manufacturing Office (AMO)**
- **Vehicle Technologies Office (VTO)**
- **Bioenergy Technologies Office (BETO)**
- **Hydrogen and Fuel Cell Technologies Office (HFTO)**

**Funding Mechanisms**:
- FOAs for specific technology areas.
- Small Business Innovation Research (SBIR).
- Technology Commercialization Fund (TCF).

### Office of Fossil Energy and Carbon Management (FECM)

**Focus**: Carbon capture, utilization, and storage (CCUS); hydrogen; critical minerals.

### Office of Nuclear Energy (NE)

**Focus**: Advanced reactor technologies, nuclear fuel cycle, university programs.

## DOE Proposal Structure

DOE proposal requirements vary significantly depending on the program office and the FOA. **Always read the specific FOA carefully.**

### Common Elements

#### Project Narrative (Length varies, typically 10-20 pages)

**Typical Structure**:

1. **Executive Summary / Abstract** (1 page)
   - Project objectives and technical approach
   - Expected outcomes and impact
   - Team qualifications
   - Alignment with DOE mission

2. **Background and Motivation** (2-3 pages)
   - Current state of technology or knowledge
   - Problem or opportunity
   - Why DOE investment is needed
   - Alignment with program goals

3. **Technical Approach and Innovation** (5-10 pages)
   - Detailed technical plan
   - Methodology and protocols
   - Innovation and novelty
   - Risk assessment and mitigation
   - Go/no-go decision points
   - Performance metrics

4. **Impact and Energy Relevance** (1-2 pages)
   - Expected technical outcomes
   - Energy impact (cost, efficiency, emissions)
   - Deployment or commercialization pathway
   - Economic benefits
   - Timeline to market (for applied programs)

5. **Management Plan** (1-2 pages)
   - Team organization and roles
   - Timeline and milestones
   - Risk management
   - Communication and reporting

6. **Qualifications and Resources** (1-2 pages)
   - Team expertise and experience
   - Relevant prior work
   - Facilities and equipment
   - National Laboratory or industry partners

#### Budget and Budget Justification

**Federal Cost Share**:
- List requested DOE funds by year.
- Breakdown by category (Labor, Equipment, Travel, etc.).
- Detailed justification for every item.

**Cost Share** (Usually mandatory):
- Specify source (Cash vs. In-kind).
- Document commitments (letters from sponsors).
- Typical requirements:
  - Universities: 20% (ARPA-E)
  - Industry: 50% or more
  - National Labs: Varies by case

**Budget Categories**:
- Personnel (Staff and their hours/rates)
- Fringe Benefits
- Travel
- Equipment and Capital Items
- Materials and Supplies
- Other Direct Costs
- Subawards/Subcontracts
- Indirect Costs (F&A)

#### Biographical Sketches

**Format**: Usually DOE-specific format or NSF-style
- Professional preparation
- Appointments
- Relevant publications (5-10 most relevant)
- Synergistic activities
- Collaborators

#### Work Breakdown Structure (WBS)

**Usually Required**: Detailed breakdown of tasks, milestones, and deliverables
- Task structure aligned with the budget
- Quarterly or annual milestones
- Deliverables for each task
- Responsible parties

#### Letters of Commitment

**Applicable to**:
- Cost-share partners
- Collaborating institutions
- National Laboratory partners
- Industry partners
- Authorization for use of facilities or resources

**Must Include**:
- Specific commitment (funds, personnel, equipment)
- Signed by an authorized representative
- On institutional letterhead

#### Facilities and Equipment

**Description**:
- Available facilities relevant to the project
- Major equipment accessible
- Computing resources
- Unique capabilities

#### Data Management Plan (DMP)

**Increasingly a hard requirement**:
- Types of data to be generated
- Standards and formats
- Access and sharing policies
- Long-term preservation
- Compliance with DOE policy

## Review Criteria

### Office of Science (SC) General Criteria

Proposals are typically evaluated based on:

1. **Scientific and/or Technical Merit** (35-40%)
   - Importance and relevance of the research
   - Appropriateness of the proposed method or approach
   - Scientific or technical innovation
   - Clarity of objectives and expected outcomes

2. **Appropriateness of the Proposed Method or Approach** (25-30%)
   - Technical feasibility
   - Likelihood of success
   - Adequacy of the project design
   - Rigor of the technical approach

3. **Competency of Personnel and Adequacy of Facilities** (20-25%)
   - Qualifications of the PI and team
   - Track record in relevant fields
   - Access to necessary facilities and equipment
   - Institutional support

4. **Reasonableness and Appropriateness of the Budget** (10-15%)
   - Budget matches proposed work
   - Appropriate allocation of resources
   - Cost-effectiveness

5. **Relevance to the DOE Mission and Program Goals** (10-15%)
   - Alignment with program priorities
   - Contribution to the DOE mission
   - Potential impact on energy/environment

### ARPA-E Review Criteria

**ARPA-E uses a "Concept Paper → Full Application" process**

**Concept Paper Review** (Typically 3-5 pages):
- Technical Innovation and Impact
- Potential for Transformative Advancement
- Relevance to Energy Applications
- Feasibility (Team, Approach)

**Full Application Review** (If invited):

1. **Impact** (40%)
   - Potential for significant improvement in energy technology
   - Energy and economic impact
   - Transformative vs. incremental
   - Path to market adoption

2. **Innovation/Technical Merit** (30%)
   - Novel approach or technology
   - Technical rigor and feasibility
   - Likelihood of achieving goals
   - Risks and risk mitigation

3. **Qualifications** (20%)
   - Team expertise and experience
   - Resources and capabilities
   - Management plan
   - Track record

4. **Work Plan** (10%)
   - Clear milestones and go/no-go decision points
   - Realistic timeline
   - Reasonable budget
   - Risk management

### Tech-to-Market (T2M) Evaluation (ARPA-E)

**Key Component**: Commercialization pathway

**Evaluation Content**:
- Market opportunity and size
- Competitive landscape
- Barriers to adoption
- Go-to-market strategy
- Partners and commercialization plan
- Economic viability

**Common Mistakes**:
- Underestimating time to market
- Ignoring competing technologies
- Unrealistic cost projections
- Lack of a clear adoption path

## DOE-Specific Considerations

### National Laboratory Collaboration

**Benefits**:
- Access to unique facilities and expertise
- Leveraging world-class capabilities
- Enhancing credibility and track record

**Mechanisms**:
- **Subcontract**: Lab acts as a subcontractor to the university/company
- **Cooperative Research and Development Agreement (CRADA)**: Partnership with industry
- **User Facility Proposal**: Requesting use of major DOE user facilities
- **Strategic Partnership Projects (SPP)**: Formal collaboration

**Process**:
- Identify suitable Lab partners early
- Contact Lab scientists to discuss collaboration
- Jointly develop scope of work and budget
- Obtain Lab approval (may take 2-3 months)
- Include letters of commitment

**Major National Laboratories**:
- Argonne (ANL), Brookhaven (BNL), Lawrence Berkeley (LBNL)
- Oak Ridge (ORNL), Pacific Northwest (PNNL), SLAC
- Sandia (SNL), Los Alamos (LANL), Lawrence Livermore (LLNL)
- National Renewable Energy Laboratory (NREL), Idaho (INL), Fermilab

### User Facilities

**DOE operates 28 major user facilities open to researchers**

**Types**:
- **Light Sources**: X-ray and neutron scattering (APS, NSLS-II, ALS, etc.)
- **Nanoscale Science Centers**: Fabrication and characterization
- **High-Performance Computing**: Supercomputing centers (OLCF, NERSC, ALCF)
- **Genomic Science**: JGI, EMSL
- **Accelerators and Detectors**: Particle and nuclear physics facilities

**Access**:
- Submit user proposals (independent of research proposals)
- Peer-reviewed allocation of beamtime or computing hours
- No fee for non-proprietary research
- Can include user facility access requests within grant proposals

### Cost Share Requirements

**Varies by program**:
- **Office of Science**: Usually not required (except for specific FOAs)
- **ARPA-E**: Mandatory (typically 20% for universities, 50%+ for industry)
- **EERE**: Usually required (depending on the program)
- **FECM**: Usually required

**Types**:
- **Cash**: Direct financial contribution
- **In-kind**: Personnel time, equipment use, materials
- **Third-party**: Contributions from collaborators or sponsors

**Requirements**:
- Must be documented and verifiable
- Cannot be used for other federal awards
- Must come from non-federal sources (usually)
- Requires letters of commitment

### Technology Readiness Levels (TRLs)

**DOE uses a TRL 1-9 scale for technology development programs**

**TRL Definitions**:
- **TRL 1-3**: Basic Research (Idea → Proof of Concept)
- **TRL 4-6**: Development (Component → System Prototype)
- **TRL 7-9**: Demonstration and Deployment (Prototype → Commercialization)

**Funding Focus by TRL**:
- **Office of Science**: TRL 1-3 (Basic Research)
- **ARPA-E**: TRL 2-5 (Proof of Concept → Prototype)
- **EERE**: TRL 4-8 (Development → Demonstration)

**Must specify in proposal**:
- Current TRL of the technology
- Target TRL at project end
- Path from current to target

### Intellectual Property and Data Rights

**Standard Terms**:
- Awardees typically retain Intellectual Property (IP)
- Government retains a license for government purposes
- Inventions must be reported to DOE
- Data sharing requirements may apply

**Industry Partners**:
- Negotiate IP and data rights early
- Protected CRADA information (5 years)
- Background IP vs. Foreground IP

### Teaming and Partnerships

**Encouraged formats**:
- University-National Lab partnerships
- University-Industry partnerships
- Multi-institutional teams
- International collaborations (requires approval)

**Teaming Partner Lists**: Programs like ARPA-E often provide teaming lists or host matchmaking events.

## Submission Process

### Finding Opportunities

**Sources**:
- **EERE Exchange**: https://eere-exchange.energy.gov
- **ARPA-E OPEN**: https://arpa-e.energy.gov
- **Office of Science FOAs**: https://science.osti.gov/grants/Funding-Opportunities
- **Grants.gov**: Federal grant database
- **FedConnect**: Subscribe to FOA announcements

### Application Systems

**Varies by office**:
- **EERE Exchange**: EERE programs
- **PAMS (Portfolio Analysis and Management System)**: Office of Science
- **ARPA-E OPEN**: ARPA-E submissions
- **Grants.gov**: Some programs

**Registration Required** (May take 2-4 weeks):
- SAM.gov (System for Award Management)
- Grants.gov
- DOE program-specific systems

### Proposal Development Timeline

**Recommended Timeline**:
- **3-6 Months Before Deadline**: Identify FOA, assemble team, contact Lab partners
- **2-3 Months**: Develop technical approach, finalize commitments
- **1-2 Months**: Draft proposal, prepare budget
- **2-4 Weeks**: Internal review, revisions
- **1 Week**: Final preparation, institutional approval
- **48 Hours Before**: Submit (do not wait until the deadline)

### Essential Registrations

**Before first submission**:
1. **SAM.gov**: System for Award Management (2-3 weeks)
2. **Grants.gov**: Account and authorization (1 week)
3. **FedConnect**: Optional, for receiving notifications
4. **PAMS/EERE Exchange**: Program-specific systems (Immediate)

**Institutional Requirements**:
- Authorized Organizational Representative (AOR)
- Internal institutional approval
- Cost accounting system

## Review and Award Process

### Timeline

**Varies by program**:
- **Office of Science**: 3-6 months
- **ARPA-E**: 4-6 months (after full application invitation)
- **EERE**: 3-6 months

**Steps**:
1. Administrative compliance check
2. Peer review (external reviewers)
3. Program Manager evaluation
4. Selection for award negotiations
5. Budget negotiation
6. Formal award

### Reviewer Feedback

**Provided Content**:
- Reviewer comments (usually anonymous)
- Strengths and weaknesses
- Scores by criteria

**Not always provided**: Some programs provide very limited feedback.

### Success Rates

**Varies widely**:
- **Office of Science Early Career Award**: ~10-15%
- **ARPA-E OPEN**: ~2-5% (from Concept Paper to final award)
- **EERE FOAs**: 10-30% (depending on specific program)
- **Office of Science FOAs**: 20-40% (case by case)

## Writing Tips to Improve DOE Proposal Competitiveness

### Do's

✅ **Align strictly with the DOE Mission** — Demonstrate relevance to energy, environment, or national security.
✅ **Emphasize Impact** — How will this advance energy technology or science?
✅ **Quantify Outcomes** — Energy savings, efficiency gains, cost reductions.
✅ **Show Deployment Path** — For applied programs, how will the tech reach the market?
✅ **Leverage DOE Capabilities** — National Labs, user facilities, unique resources.
✅ **Include a Strong Management Plan** — Milestones, go/no-go points, risk mitigation.
✅ **Demonstrate Team Qualifications** — Track record in relevant fields.
✅ **Clearly State Innovation** — What is new and why does it matter.
✅ **Address TRLs** — Current TRL and the path forward.
✅ **Secure Cost-Share Commitments** — If required, get letters early.

### Don'ts

❌ **Don't ignore FOA requirements** — Every FOA is different; read carefully.
❌ **Don't underestimate the timeline** — Allow time for registration and approvals.
❌ **Don't forget cost share** — If required, it must be documented.
❌ **Don't overlook Lab collaborations** — These can significantly strengthen a proposal.
❌ **Don't be vague on impact** — Quantitative energy/economic metrics are needed.
❌ **Don't ignore commercialization** — For applied programs, the market path is vital.
❌ **Don't submit without institutional approval** — Requires AOR signature.
❌ **Don't wait until the deadline** — Systems crash; submit 48 hours early.
❌ **Don't submit basic science to ARPA-E** — Or applied research to the Office of Science.
❌ **Don't forget to discuss TRL** — This is critical for technical plans.

### Common Mistakes

1. **Wrong Program**: Submitting to an inappropriate office or program.
2. **Insufficient Energy Relevance**: Failing to link clearly to the DOE mission.
3. **Weak Commercialization Plan**: For ARPA-E and EERE, lacking a market strategy.
4. **Unrealistic Milestones**: Overly optimistic timelines.
5. **Poor Budget Justification**: Budget doesn't match the technical plan.
6. **Missing Cost Share**: Failing to provide proper documentation if required.
7. **Weak Team**: Lacking sufficient expertise or track record.
8. **Ignoring Competition**: Failing to analyze the competitive landscape.

## Recent DOE Priorities (2024-2025)

### Focus Areas

- **Clean Energy Transition**: Renewables, energy storage, grid modernization.
- **Carbon Management**: Carbon capture, utilization, storage, and removal.
- **Critical Materials**: Supply chain security, recycling, alternatives.
- **Advanced Manufacturing**: Energy-efficient processes, sustainable materials.
- **Quantum Information Science**: Computing, sensing, communication.
- **Fusion Energy**: Accelerating fusion development.
- **Hydrogen Economy**: Production, storage, utilization.
- **Nuclear Energy**: Advanced reactors, micro-reactors, fuel cycle.
- **Climate Adaptation**: Climate modeling, resilience, impact.
- **Energy Equity**: Environmental justice, workforce development.

### Major Initiatives

- **Energy Earthshots**: Ambitious R&D goals (Hydrogen Shot, Long Duration Storage Shot, Carbon Negative Shot, etc.).
- **Bipartisan Infrastructure Law (BIL)**: $62 billion for DOE programs.
- **Inflation Reduction Act (IRA)**: Clean energy tax credits and programs.
- **CHIPS and Science Act**: Microelectronics, quantum, clean energy manufacturing.
