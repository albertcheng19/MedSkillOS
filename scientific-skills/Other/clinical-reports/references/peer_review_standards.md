# Peer Review Standards for Clinical Manuscripts

## Overview of Peer Review for Clinical Manuscripts

### Purpose

Peer review aims to ensure that clinical manuscripts meet standards of scientific rigor, ethical compliance, and clear communication before publication.

**Objectives:**
- Assess scientific validity and methodology
- Evaluate clinical significance
- Verify ethical compliance
- Ensure clarity and completeness
- Improve manuscript quality

**Types of Peer Review:**
- Single-blind (Reviewers know authors, authors do not know reviewers)
- Double-blind (Both parties are anonymous)
- Open peer review (Both parties' identities are public)
- Post-publication peer review

### Reviewer Responsibilities

**Accept review assignments only if you:**
- Possess professional qualifications in the subject area
- Have no conflicts of interest
- Have sufficient time (usually 2-3 weeks)
- Can provide constructive, unbiased evaluations

**Maintain Confidentiality:**
- Do not share manuscript content
- Do not use information for personal gain
- Do not involve others without editorial permission

**Provide Timely Reviews:**
- Complete within the requested timeframe
- Notify the editor promptly if unable to complete

## Review Guidelines for Case Reports

### CARE Guideline Compliance

**Verify if the manuscript includes:**
- [ ] Title identifying it as a case report
- [ ] Keywords (2-5)
- [ ] Structured or unstructured abstract
- [ ] Introduction explaining the novelty of the case
- [ ] Patient information (de-identified)
- [ ] Clinical findings
- [ ] Timeline of events
- [ ] Diagnostic assessment
- [ ] Therapeutic intervention
- [ ] Follow-up and outcomes
- [ ] Discussion including a literature review
- [ ] Patient perspective (if applicable)
- [ ] Informed consent statement

### Novelty and Significance

**Assess:**
- Is the case truly novel, or does it add to medical knowledge?
- Why is this case worth reporting?
- Is the disease rare, or is the presentation unusual?
- Does it challenge existing knowledge?
- Are there clinical lessons that can be generalized?

**Red Flags:**
- Common presentation of a common disease
- Single case without unique features
- Overly generalized conclusions from a single case
- Lack of literature review to prove novelty

### Privacy and Ethical Considerations

**Verify:**
- Informed consent has been obtained and documented
- Patient is sufficiently de-identified (removal of 18 HIPAA identifiers)
- No identifiable images without explicit consent
- Dates are removed or approximated
- Geographic information is limited to province/country
- Age is described appropriately (exact age or range)
- Institutional identifiers are removed

**Ethical Issues:**
- Missing informed consent documentation
- Presence of identifiable information
- Retrospective chart reviews lacking IRB approval (if applicable)
- Lack of additional protections for vulnerable populations

### Clinical Quality

**Diagnostic Process:**
- Appropriate workup for presenting symptoms
- Differential diagnosis considered
- Logical derivation of the final diagnosis
- Adequate documentation of findings

**Treatment:**
- Evidence-based interventions
- Rationale for treatment choices
- Alternative options considered
- Appropriate monitoring and follow-up

**Outcomes:**
- Clear description of clinical results
- Appropriate follow-up duration
- Documentation of complications
- Long-term outcomes provided (if available)

### Literature Review

**Assess:**
- Adequate search of existing literature
- Identification and discussion of similar cases
- Review of current understanding of the condition
- Appropriate contextualization of the case
- References are current and relevant
- Comparison with previous cases

### Writing Quality

**Structure:**
- Logical flow and well-organized
- Follows CARE guideline structure
- Writing is clear and concise
- Proper use of medical terminology

**Clarity:**
- Medical jargon is explained
- Timeline is clear and easy to understand
- Logical sequence of events
- Conclusions are supported by case details

## Review Guidelines for Clinical Trial Manuscripts

### Study Design and Methodology

**Assess:**
- Appropriate study design for the research question
- Clear objectives and hypotheses
- Well-defined primary and secondary endpoints
- Adequate sample size with power calculation
- Proper use of randomization and blinding
- Reasonable control group setting

**Red Flags:**
- Post-hoc changes to endpoints
- Underpowered studies claiming equivalence
- Inappropriate statistical methods
- Lack of blinding where feasible
- Selection bias in the enrollment process

### CONSORT Compliance

**Verify:**
- Title identifies it as a randomized trial
- Structured abstract
- Trial registration number provided
- Protocol availability
- Inclusion of a CONSORT flow diagram
- Baseline characteristics table
- Reporting of all results (not just significant ones)
- Reporting of adverse events
- Disclosure of funding sources
- Statement of conflicts of interest

### Randomization and Allocation

**Assess:**
- Adequate sequence generation method
- Proper allocation concealment
- Balanced baseline characteristics
- Clear stratification factors
- Documentation of crossovers and protocol deviations

### Participant Flow

**Verify:**
- Number of participants screened reported
- Reasons for exclusion provided
- Number randomized is clear
- Number of withdrawals and reasons documented
- Loss to follow-up minimized and explained
- Clear ITT (Intention-to-Treat) and PP (Per-Protocol) analyses
- CONSORT diagram is complete and accurate

### Outcome Measures

**Primary Outcomes:**
- Pre-defined and clearly defined
- Clinically meaningful
- Appropriate for the research question
- Reliable and valid measurement
- Proper statistical analysis

**Secondary Outcomes:**
- Pre-specified in the protocol
- Appropriately analyzed
- Correction for multiple comparisons if necessary
- Not over-interpreted if underpowered

**Exploratory Outcomes:**
- Clearly labeled as exploratory or post-hoc
- Should not be weighted equally with primary outcomes
- Used for hypothesis generation, not confirmatory conclusions

### Statistical Analysis

**Assess:**
- Analysis plan determined before unblinding
- Correct statistical tests used
- Verification of assumptions (normality, etc.)
- Proper handling of missing data
- Multiplicity adjustments where necessary
- Confidence intervals provided
- Effect sizes reported

**Common Issues:**
- P-hacking (selective reporting)
- Uncorrected multiple testing
- Inappropriate subgroup analyses
- Arbitrary switching between ITT and PP analyses
- Ignoring or improperly handling missing data

### Safety Reporting

**Verify:**
- All adverse events reported
- Serious adverse events detailed
- Complete description of deaths
- Causality assessment performed
- Laboratory abnormalities reported
- Documentation of discontinuations due to adverse events

### Clinical Significance

**Assess:**
- Statistical significance vs. clinical significance
- Magnitude of effect is clinically meaningful
- Number Needed to Treat (NNT, if applicable)
- Favorable benefit-risk ratio
- Generalizability to practice
- Cost-effectiveness considerations

## Review Guidelines for Diagnostic Studies

### STARD Guidelines (Standards for Reporting Diagnostic Accuracy)

**Assess Compliance:**
- Study design described
- Participant selection criteria
- Sampling method
- Data collection procedures
- Reference standard (gold standard) defined
- Index test described in detail
- Blinding mentioned
- Clear participant flow
- 2x2 table provided
- Diagnostic accuracy estimates

### Reference Standard

**Verify:**
- Appropriate gold standard used
- Same reference standard used for all participants
- Reference standard performed regardless of index test results
- Appropriate time interval between index test and reference standard
- Independent determination of index test and reference standard results

### Test Performance

**Essential Metrics:**
- Sensitivity and Specificity
- Positive and Negative Predictive Values (contextualized with prevalence)
- Likelihood ratios
- ROC curves and AUC (for continuous outcomes)
- 95% confidence intervals for all estimates

**Considerations:**
- Pre-test and post-test probability
- Clinical utility beyond accuracy
- Comparison with existing tests
- Cost and availability

### Spectrum Bias and Verification Bias

**Assess:**
- Inclusion of a spectrum of disease severity
- Avoidance of spectrum bias (including only severe cases)
- Avoidance of verification bias (all participants receive the reference standard)
- Avoidance of differential verification (different reference standards for different participants)

## Review Guidelines for Observational Studies

### STROBE Guidelines (Strengthening the Reporting of Observational Studies in Epidemiology)

**For cohort, case-control, or cross-sectional studies, verify:**
- Title and abstract identify the study design
- Clear background and rationale
- Objectives clearly stated
- Methods reflect the study design
- Setting described
- Participants described
- Variables clearly defined
- Data sources and measurement methods detailed
- Bias addressed
- Study size justification
- Statistical methods described
- Effect sizes and confidence intervals (CIs) reported

### Exposure and Outcome Assessment

**Assess:**
- Clear definition of exposure
- Clear definition of outcome
- Valid and reliable measurement methods
- Blinding of assessors where possible
- Consistent measurement across groups
- Appropriate temporal relationship between exposure and outcome

### Confounding and Bias

**Verify:**
- Potential confounders identified
- Adjustment for confounders in the analysis
- Discussion of residual confounding
- Selection bias addressed
- Information bias considered
- Sensitivity analyses performed

### Causality

**Bradford Hill Criteria Considerations:**
- Strength of association
- Consistency across studies
- Specificity
- Temporality (exposure precedes outcome)
- Biological gradient (dose-response)
- Plausibility
- Coherence with existing knowledge
- Experimental evidence
- Analogy

**Avoid:**
- Using causal language for observational studies without strong evidence
- Confusing association with causation

## Review Guidelines for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses

### PRISMA Guidelines

**Verify:**
- Title identifies it as a systematic review/meta-analysis
- Structured abstract
- Research question (PICO format)
- Protocol and registration (PROSPERO)
- Comprehensive search strategy
- Description of study selection process
- Data extraction process
- Quality assessment of included studies
- Appropriate synthesis methods
- Results include forest plots
- Heterogeneity assessment
- Publication bias assessed
- Certainty of evidence (GRADE)

### Search Strategy

**Assess:**
- Multiple databases searched
- Comprehensive search terms
- Reasonable limits and filters
- Grey literature considered
- Hand-searching of references
- Contacting authors for missing data
- Search is reproducible

### Study Selection

**Verify:**
- Pre-specified inclusion/exclusion criteria
- Independent screening by ≥2 reviewers
- Disagreements resolved appropriately
- PRISMA flow diagram complete
- List of excluded studies with reasons

### Quality Assessment

**Assess:**
- Appropriate quality assessment tools used
  - RCTs: Cochrane Risk of Bias tool
  - Observational studies: Newcastle-Ottawa Scale
  - Diagnostic studies: QUADAS-2
- Independent quality assessment
- Quality assessment results reported
- Quality factors incorporated into synthesis

### Statistical Methods

**For Meta-Analyses:**
- Rationale for Fixed-effect vs. Random-effects model
- Heterogeneity assessed (I², Q statistic)
- Forest plots provided
- Publication bias assessed (Funnel plot, Egger’s test)
- Sensitivity analyses performed
- Subgroup analyses pre-specified

### GRADE Assessment

**Certainty of Evidence:**
- High: Very confident in the effect estimate
- Moderate: Moderately confident
- Low: Limited confidence
- Very Low: Little to no confidence

**Factors Decreasing Certainty:**
- Risk of bias
- Inconsistency
- Indirectness
- Imprecision
- Publication bias

## Manuscript Quality Assessment

### Structure and Organization

**Assess:**
- Logical flow from Introduction to Discussion
- Well-organized sections
- Figures and tables support the text
- Appropriate supplementary materials

### Writing Quality

**Clarity:**
- Language is clear and concise
- Jargon is minimized and defined
- Abbreviations defined at first use
- Consistent terminology

**Grammar and Style:**
- Correct grammar and spelling
- Appropriate verb tenses (past tense for results, present tense for established facts)
- Proper use of active voice
- Conciseness without sacrificing clarity

### References

**Verify:**
- Sufficient number of references
- Inclusion of current literature
- Key papers cited
- Correct reference formatting
- All in-text citations appear in the list and vice versa
- No excessive self-citation

### Figures and Tables

**Assess:**
- Appropriate for the data type
- Clear labels and legends
- High image quality
- Self-explanatory (understandable without the main text)
- Non-redundant with the text
- Correct statistical symbols

## Ethical Considerations in Peer Review

### Conflicts of Interest

**Disclose and recuse if:**
- Personal relationship with authors
- Financial interest in the outcome
- Competing research
- Strong bias for or against the topic
- Institutional conflict

### Fair and Constructive Review

**Provide:**
- Balanced assessment of strengths and weaknesses
- Specific, actionable suggestions
- Respectful tone
- Objective evaluation
- Recognition of study design limitations

**Avoid:**
- Personal attacks
- Dismissive language
- Requesting unreasonable revisions
- Expecting a perfect study
- Imposing personal preferences over standards

### Confidentiality

**Maintain:**
- Do not share the manuscript
- Do not discuss with colleagues without permission
- Do not use ideas or data
- Destroy copies after the review is complete

## Categories of Recommendation

**Accept:**
- Manuscript meets publication standards
- Only minor editing required

**Minor Revisions:**
- Small issues that can be addressed
- No additional data needed
- Usually one round of revision

**Major Revisions:**
- Significant concerns requiring substantial changes
- May need additional analysis
- May need additional data or experiments
- Usually requires re-review

**Reject:**
- Fundamental flaws that cannot be corrected
- Insufficient novelty or significance
- Violation of ethical standards
- Data fabrication

**Reject and Resubmit:**
- Study has potential but requires extensive work
- Substantially a new submission after major changes

## Writing the Review Report

### Structure

**Summary:**
- Brief overview (2-3 sentences)
- Overall assessment
- Major strengths (2-3 points)
- Major weaknesses (2-3 points)
- Recommendation

**Major Comments (Numbered):**
- Listed by number
- Significant issues affecting validity, interpretation, or impact
- Specific and actionable
- Prioritized by importance

**Minor Comments (Numbered):**
- Listed by number
- Editorial, formatting, or clarification issues
- Notes on specific line numbers
- Notes on figures and tables

### Tone and Language

**Use:**
- Professional, peer-to-peer tone
- "The authors state..." instead of "You state..."
- "This study shows..." instead of "Your study shows..."
- Constructive criticism
- Suggestions for improvement

**Avoid:**
- Harsh or dismissive language
- Personal pronouns (e.g., "you")
- Sarcasm
- Vague criticism
- Unreasonable demands

### Specific and Actionable Feedback

**Good:**
"The sample size calculation (page 8) does not account for the expected loss to follow-up. Please revise to include the anticipated attrition and explain how this affects the enrollment target."

**Bad:**
"The sample size is insufficient."

**Good:**
"It would be clearer if the error bars in Figure 2 represented the 95% CI instead of the SEM. Please revise accordingly and update the legend."

**Bad:**
"Figure 2 is confusing."

This reference document provides comprehensive peer review standards for clinical manuscripts, covering case reports, clinical trials, diagnostic studies, observational studies, and systematic reviews. Please use these guidelines to conduct thorough and constructive peer reviews.
