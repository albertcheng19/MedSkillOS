# Broader Impacts: Strategies and Best Practices

## Overview

**Broader Impacts** is one of the two merit review criteria for National Science Foundation (NSF) proposals, carrying equal weight with "Intellectual Merit." Despite this, Broader Impacts are often treated as an afterthought—a critical mistake that frequently leads to otherwise strong proposals losing funding.

**NSF Definition**: "The potential to benefit society and contribute to the achievement of specific, desired societal outcomes."

**Core Principle**: Broader Impacts must be **specific, measurable, and integrated with your research plan**, rather than a vague vision tacked onto the end.

## The Five Pillars of Broader Impacts

The NSF evaluates Broader Impacts across five primary areas. **You do not need to cover all five**, but you should substantively address at least one through specific activities, timelines, and evaluation plans.

### 1. Advance Discovery and Understanding While Promoting Teaching, Training, and Learning

**What it means**: Integrating research with education to inspire the next generation of scientists and improve scientific literacy.

**Effective Strategies**:

**Curriculum Development**:
- Creating new courses that incorporate research findings
- Developing course modules or laboratory exercises
- Designing online learning materials (MOOCs, videos, interactive tools)
- Contributing to textbooks or educational resources

*Example*: "We will develop a 10-week computational biology module for undergraduate education, incorporating real-world datasets from this project. The module will include Jupyter notebooks with guided analysis, video tutorials, and assessment tools. These materials will be piloted at our institution (reaching 50 students/year) and made freely available for national adoption via CourseSource."

**Student Training**:
- Undergraduate Research Experiences (REU)
- Graduate student mentoring
- Postdoctoral training
- High school internship programs
- Research Experiences for Teachers (RET)

*Example*: "This project will support 3 PhD students and 6 undergraduate researchers over 5 years. Undergraduates will participate through our existing summer research program (10 weeks, $5,000 stipend) and will present findings at the annual undergraduate research symposium and regional conferences."

**Instructional Innovation**:
- Problem-based learning modules
- Active learning strategies
- Research-intensive courses
- Service-learning projects
- Makerspaces or hands-on workshops

*Example*: "We will transform our introductory physics course (250 students/year) by implementing studio-style physics instruction based on the findings of this research. The new curriculum will include 3D visualization tools for electromagnetic fields, inquiry-based problem sets, and peer instruction protocols."

**Professional Development**:
- Workshops for faculty or teachers
- Training programs for early-career researchers
- Mentoring programs
- Career development resources

*Example*: "We will host an annual 3-day workshop for 25 community college faculty members, providing training in genome editing techniques. Participants will gain hands-on experience with CRISPR methods developed in this project, complete a teaching module for their courses, and receive ongoing support through a virtual learning community."

### 2. Broaden Participation of Underrepresented Groups

**What it means**: Increasing the representation of groups currently underrepresented in STEM fields, including women, minorities, persons with disabilities, and those from economically disadvantaged backgrounds.

**Effective Strategies**:

**Partnerships with Minority Serving Institutions (MSIs)**:
- Collaborating with HBCUs (Historically Black Colleges and Universities)
- Partnering with HSIs (Hispanic Serving Institutions)
- Engaging with TCUs (Tribal Colleges and Universities)
- Working with community colleges

*Example*: "We will establish a formal research partnership with four regional HBCUs (North Carolina A&T, Howard University, Morehouse College, and Spelman College). Each summer, two students from partner institutions will participate in a 10-week research internship, including a stipend ($6,000), housing, travel to the site, and participation in our weekly research seminar series. Faculty liaisons at each partner institution will co-mentor students and facilitate year-round engagement."

**Recruitment and Retention**:
- Targeted recruitment at conferences (SACNAS, ABRCMS, NSBE, SWE)
- Scholarship programs for underrepresented students
- Bridge programs for community college transfer students
- Retention support (mentoring, peer networks, professional development)

*Example*: "We will recruit 50% of our summer undergraduate researchers from groups underrepresented in computer science through partnerships with SACNAS and the National Society of Black Engineers. Participants will be mentored by graduate students with similar backgrounds, attend professional development workshops, and join our Computing Diversity Learning Community, which provides year-round support and networking."

**Culturally Relevant Engagement**:
- Research addressing community-identified needs
- Community-based participatory research
- Engagement with Indigenous communities
- Bilingual materials and outreach

*Example*: "In partnership with the Navajo Nation, we will conduct participatory research on water quality in reservation communities. Community members will co-design research questions, participate in data collection, and contribute Indigenous knowledge regarding local hydrology. Results will be shared through community briefings in both English and Navajo and will inform tribal water management policies."

**Removing Systemic Barriers**:
- Flexible scheduling for non-traditional students
- Childcare support for participants
- Accessible facilities and materials
- Financial support (stipends, travel, equipment)
- Mentoring networks and affinity groups

*Example*: "To support participation from students from low-income backgrounds, we will provide laptops, software licenses, and internet hotspots for all research participants. We will also offer flexible work schedules, remote participation options, and supplemental funding for students with childcare or eldercare responsibilities."

### 3. Enhance Infrastructure for Research and Education

**What it means**: Building facilities, tools, databases, or networks that enable future research and education for the broader community.

**Effective Strategies**:

**Shared Research Infrastructure**:
- Multi-user instrumentation
- Core facilities
- Field stations or observatories
- Computational resources
- Cyberinfrastructure

*Example*: "We will establish a regional Cryo-EM facility serving 15 institutions across the Southwest. The facility will provide training and state-of-the-art imaging capabilities currently unavailable in the region. We will operate a user program with subsidized rates for academic users and provide annual training workshops for 50 researchers."

**Data and Software Resources**:
- Open-access databases
- Software tools and platforms
- Analysis pipelines
- Standardized protocols
- Data repositories

*Example*: "We will develop and maintain EcoDataHub, an open-source platform for ecological time-series analysis. The platform will include automated data cleaning, standardized analysis workflows, interactive visualization tools, and cloud computing integration. The software will be documented, version-controlled on GitHub, and supported through user forums and quarterly webinars. Based on community surveys, we anticipate over 1,000 users within 3 years."

**Biological or Physical Resources**:
- Living resource centers (model organisms, cell lines)
- Herbarium collections
- Reagent banks
- Seed banks or tissue collections

*Example*: "We will establish a publicly accessible repository of 500 sequenced bacterial strains isolated from extreme environments. Each strain will include full genome sequences, phenotypic characterization, and growth protocols. Materials will be made available through ATCC, with metadata deposited in NCBI BioProject."

**Standards and Protocols**:
- Community standards
- Best practice guidelines
- Benchmark datasets
- Quality control metrics
- Interoperability frameworks

*Example*: "Through a collaboration with 20 international laboratories, we will develop and validate standardized protocols for single-cell RNA sequencing analysis. The resulting guidelines will address batch effects, quality control, normalization methods, and statistical best practices. Protocols will be published in peer-reviewed literature and deposited on protocols.io."

### 4. Broaden Dissemination to Enhance Scientific and Technological Understanding

**What it means**: Communicating research to a wider audience, including the general public, K-12 students, policymakers, and stakeholders, to enhance scientific literacy and informed decision-making.

**Effective Strategies**:

**K-12 Educational Outreach**:
- School visits and science demonstrations
- After-school programs
- Science fairs and competitions
- Teacher professional development
- Classroom resources and lesson plans

*Example*: "We will partner with 10 local middle schools (serving 75% low-income students) to conduct hands-on robotics workshops. Each school will receive robotics kits, and we will train teachers to lead a 12-week after-school robotics club. Students will apply concepts from this research (sensor fusion, autonomous navigation) to design robots that address real-world challenges. The program will reach 200 students annually."

**Public Engagement**:
- Museum partnerships and exhibits
- Science cafes and public lectures
- Science festivals
- Citizen science projects
- Community workshops

*Example*: "We will collaborate with the Museum of Science and Industry to create a permanent interactive exhibit on climate modeling. The exhibit will allow visitors to manipulate climate variables and observe predicted outcomes using a simplified version of our model. We anticipate 500,000 annual visitors. We will also host quarterly 'Climate Science Saturday' public lectures, reaching 2,000 community members per year."

**Media and Communication**:
- Blog posts and articles
- Podcasts or videos
- Social media engagement
- Press releases for major findings
- Popular science writing

*Example*: "We will produce a 6-episode podcast series exploring the intersection of AI and creativity, featuring interviews with artists, musicians, and computer scientists. Episodes will be available for free on major platforms, with transcripts and educational materials provided on our website. Based on our existing podcast (15,000 downloads/episode), we expect to reach 100,000+ listeners."

**Policy Engagement**:
- Science policy fellowships
- Congressional briefings
- White papers for policymakers
- Stakeholder workshops
- Contributions to regulatory science

*Example*: "We will organize an annual symposium bringing together researchers, water utility operators, environmental regulators, and community advocates to discuss the implications of our research for drinking water policy. Findings will be summarized in policy briefs distributed to state and federal agencies. The PI will participate in the AAAS Science & Technology Policy Fellowship to engage directly with EPA rulemaking."

**Citizen Science**:
- Community-based data collection
- Participatory research design
- Volunteer monitoring programs
- Crowdsourcing platforms

*Example*: "We will launch a citizen science project recruiting 500 volunteers across the Midwest to monitor pollinator populations using our smartphone app. Participants will receive training materials, identification guides, and regular feedback on their observations. Data will contribute directly to our research while building public understanding of pollinator ecology. Results will be visualized on an interactive public dashboard."

### 5. Benefit Society

**What it means**: Applying research to address societal needs, improve quality of life, strengthen national security, or enhance economic competitiveness.

**Effective Strategies**:

**Health and Well-being**:
- Clinical applications
- Public health improvements
- Healthcare accessibility
- Mental health resources
- Environmental health

*Example*: "Our diagnostic tool will reduce the cost of malaria testing from $10 to $0.50, enabling deployment in resource-limited settings. We will partner with PATH and Doctors Without Borders to conduct field trials in three African countries and establish manufacturing partnerships for large-scale production. We anticipate this technology could benefit 10 million patients annually within 5 years."

**Economic Development**:
- Technology commercialization
- Job creation
- Industry partnerships
- Workforce development
- Startup incubation

*Example*: "We will establish an industry partnership program with five regional manufacturing companies to transfer our advanced material synthesis methods. Through quarterly technical workshops and on-site consultations, we will help companies integrate these processes into their production lines, potentially creating 50-100 high-skilled jobs within 5 years. Two graduate students will complete internships at partner companies."

**Environmental Sustainability**:
- Climate change mitigation or adaptation
- Conservation and biodiversity
- Pollution reduction
- Sustainable agriculture
- Renewable energy

*Example*: "We will work with 15 Iowa farmers to implement our soil carbon sequestration practices on 1,000 acres of farmland. We will provide training, monitoring support, and access to carbon credit markets. If successful, scaling these practices to 10% of Midwest farmland could sequester 100,000 tons of CO2 equivalent annually while increasing farmer income by $50-$100 per acre through carbon credits."

**National and Homeland Security**:
- Defense applications
- Cybersecurity
- Critical infrastructure protection
- Emergency response
- Intelligence capabilities

*Example*: "We will collaborate with the Department of Homeland Security to adapt our threat detection algorithms for use in transportation security screening. The technology will be piloted at three major airports, aiming to reduce false alarm rates by 40% while maintaining security effectiveness, thereby decreasing passenger wait times and improving screening efficiency."

**Social and Cultural Benefits**:
- Preservation of cultural heritage
- Accessibility and inclusion
- Social justice
- Arts and humanities
- Quality of life improvements

*Example*: "Our 3D scanning and virtual reality platform will be used to digitally preserve 20 culturally significant sites threatened by climate change and development. Virtual reconstructions will be made available for free to descendant communities, schools, and the public through a web interface and VR experiences. We will partner with Indigenous groups to ensure the appropriateness of cultural representations."

## Best Practices for Broader Impacts

### Be Specific and Concrete

**Vague** ❌:
"This research will train the next generation of scientists."

**Specific** ✅:
"This project will support 3 PhD students, 2 postdocs, and 12 undergraduate researchers over 5 years. Undergraduates will be recruited through our partnership with the Louis Stokes Alliance for Minority Participation, with a target of 50% participation from underrepresented groups. Students will receive training in advanced microscopy, data analysis, and science communication, and will present their research at the annual Emerging Researchers National Conference."

### Include Timelines and Milestones

**Vague** ❌:
"We will develop educational materials."

**Specific** ✅:
"Year 1: Develop draft curriculum modules and pilot with 50 students.
Year 2: Revise based on assessment data and expand to 150 students across 3 institutions.
Years 3-5: National dissemination via CourseSource, workshops at 2 professional conferences, and establishment of an online repository. Goal: Adoption by 20 institutions reaching 1,000 students annually by Year 5."

### Measure and Evaluate Impact

**Should include**:
- Quantitative metrics (number of participants, downloads, users)
- Qualitative assessments (surveys, interviews, focus groups)
- Learning outcomes or behavioral changes
- Longitudinal tracking
- Comparison with baseline or control groups

**Example**:
"We will evaluate the effectiveness of the program through: (1) pre/post-surveys of science self-efficacy using validated instruments, (2) tracking participant persistence in STEM majors through institutional records, (3) focus groups with participants and faculty, and (4) analysis of student work products. We anticipate a 30% increase in science self-efficacy scores and a 90% retention rate in STEM majors among participants, compared to an institutional baseline of 65%."

### Leverage Existing Infrastructure

**Don't reinvent the wheel**—build on existing programs and partnerships:
- Institutional programs (REU sites, AGEP, LSAMP, etc.)
- Established community partnerships
- Shared facilities or resources
- Professional societies and organizations

*Example*: "We will integrate with our institution's existing NSF Materials Science REU site, adding 2 positions focused on our research area. This leverages established recruitment pipelines, professional development programming, and evaluation infrastructure with 15 partner institutions while expanding opportunities for undergraduate researchers."

### Demonstrate Institutional Commitment

**Show that Broader Impacts will live beyond the grant period**:
- Institutional cost-sharing or support
- Integration into ongoing programs
- Sustainability plans
- Letters of commitment from partners

*Example*: "The University has committed $50,000 annually in cost-sharing to maintain the high school outreach program beyond the grant period. The program will be integrated into our Center for STEM Education, ensuring administrative support, space, and continuity. Our partner school district has committed teacher time and classroom access (see Letter of Commitment in Supplementary Documents)."

### Align with the Research Plan

**Integration Example**:
- Students work on research questions from the proposal
- Educational materials use data generated by the research
- Outreach activities disseminate research findings
- Community needs guide the research questions

**Poor Integration** ❌:
Quantum computing research + Unrelated middle school marine biology outreach

**Good Integration** ✅:
Quantum computing research + Developing quantum computing curriculum modules + Summer program for students to program quantum simulators + Public lectures on quantum technology

## Common Mistakes in Broader Impacts

### Mistake 1: General and Vague

❌ "This project will train graduate students and postdocs."
❌ "Results will be disseminated widely through publications and conferences."
❌ "We will engage in outreach activities."

These are baseline expectations, not Broader Impacts.

### Mistake 2: No Plan or Timeline

❌ "We hope to develop educational materials that can be used nationally."

✅ "Year 1: Develop and pilot 5 teaching modules. Year 2: Evaluate effectiveness and refine. Year 3: Publish in Journal of Chemical Education. Years 4-5: Disseminate via workshops at 3 national conferences and an online repository. Goal: 30 institutional adoptions by Year 5."

### Mistake 3: No Evaluation

❌ "We will host a summer camp for underrepresented students."

✅ "We will host a 4-week summer camp for 30 students (60% from underrepresented groups). We will evaluate impact through pre/post-content knowledge tests, science identity surveys, and tracking STEM course enrollment. We expect 80% of participants to enroll in advanced science courses the following year."

### Mistake 4: Unrealistic Scope

❌ "We will build a national network of 100 schools, develop a comprehensive K-12 curriculum, create a museum exhibit, launch a national citizen science project, and commercialize our technology" (with no budget or personnel allocated).

Be realistic about what can be accomplished with the available resources and time.

### Mistake 5: Poor Integration

❌ Plant genomics research + Unrelated robotics outreach

✅ Plant genomics research + Developing plant biology curriculum + Engaging community gardens in phenology citizen science

### Mistake 6: Treated as an Afterthought

❌ A half-page of generic statements at the end of the proposal with no budget allocation.

✅ Integrated throughout the proposal, with dedicated personnel (0.5 months PI time, 10% graduate student, summer coordinator), allocated budget ($15k/year), and a detailed plan and evaluation strategy.

### Mistake 7: No Track Record

Reviewers are skeptical if you propose extensive Broader Impacts activities with no history of such work.

✅ Demonstrate preliminary efforts, leverage existing programs, include collaborators with relevant expertise, and cite previous successful Broader Impacts work.

## Budgeting for Broader Impacts

**The NSF expects resources to be allocated to Broader Impacts activities.**

**Typical Budget Items**:
- **Personnel**: Project coordinators, graduate students, undergraduate assistants
- **Participant Support**: Stipends, travel, and housing for students/teachers
- **Materials and Supplies**: Educational materials, outreach equipment, workshop supplies
- **Travel**: Conference presentations for Broader Impacts work, field visits to partners
- **Subawards**: Payments to partner institutions or organizations
- **Evaluation**: External evaluators for assessment

**Budget Example**:
- Summer Program Coordinator (2 months/year): $15,000/year
- Undergraduate Stipends (10 students × $5,000): $50,000/year
- Workshop Materials and Supplies: $5,000/year
- Travel for Recruitment and Partner Meetings: $3,000/year
- External Evaluator: $8,000/year
- **Total: $81,000/year (16% of a $500k budget)**

---

**Core Takeaway**: Effective Broader Impacts are specific, measurable, evaluated, integrated with the research plan, and demonstrate institutional commitment. Plan Broader Impacts with the same rigor as the research itself, with dedicated resources, timelines, milestones, and evaluation strategies. Generic statements about "training students" or "disseminating results" are insufficient—the NSF expects a concrete plan that demonstrates a benefit to society.
