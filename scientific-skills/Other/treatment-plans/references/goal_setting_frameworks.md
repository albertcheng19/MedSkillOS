# Goal Setting Framework for Treatment Plans

## Overview

Effective treatment goals are the cornerstone of successful patient care. This reference guide provides comprehensive guidance on creating SMART goals, selecting patient-centered outcomes, and the shared decision-making process for treatment planning across all medical specialties.

## SMART Goals Framework

### Definition

**SMART** is an acronym for guidelines that ensure goals are well-defined and reachable:
- **S**pecific
- **M**easurable
- **A**chievable
- **R**elevant
- **T**ime-bound

### 1. Specific

Goals must be clear, explicit, and unambiguous.

**Components of Specificity**:
- **What**: Exactly what is to be accomplished
- **Who**: Who is responsible (patient, provider, or both)
- **Where**: Relevant context or setting
- **Which**: Specific aspect of health/function being addressed

**Examples**:

| Poor (Vague) | Good (Specific) |
|--------------|-----------------|
| "Feel better" | "Reduce depressive symptoms as measured by the PHQ-9 score" |
| "Improve diabetes" | "Lower HbA1c from current 8.5% to below 7%" |
| "Get stronger" | "Increase right quadriceps strength from 3/5 to 4/5 on Manual Muscle Testing" |
| "Lose weight" | "Lose 10 lbs (from 210 lbs to 200 lbs)" |
| "Exercise more" | "Walk for 30 minutes, 5 days per week" |

### 2. Measurable

Goals must contain quantifiable metrics or observable criteria to track progress.

**Types of Measurement**:
- **Quantitative**: Numbers, percentages, scores, scales
  - Lab values: HbA1c, LDL cholesterol, eGFR
  - Vital signs: Blood pressure, heart rate, weight
  - Scales: Pain (0-10 NRS), PHQ-9, GAD-7, FIM
  - Function: Walking distance, ROM degrees, strength grading
  
- **Qualitative Observable**: Behaviors that can be seen and verified
  - "Patient demonstrates correct insulin injection technique"
  - "Patient ambulates 150 feet independently with a rolling walker"
  - "Patient follows two-step commands"

**Examples**:

| Not Measurable | Measurable |
|----------------|------------|
| "Better blood pressure" | "Systolic BP <130 mmHg and Diastolic BP <80 mmHg" |
| "Less pain" | "Reduce pain intensity from 7/10 to ≤4/10 on the Numerical Rating Scale" |
| "Improved mobility" | "Walk 300 feet with a front-wheeled walker under supervision" |
| "Take meds regularly" | "Medication adherence >90% as measured by pharmacy refill rates" |
| "Sleep better" | "Sleep 7-8 hours per night with <2 nocturnal awakenings" |

### 3. Achievable

Goals must be realistic given the patient's abilities, resources, and environment.

**Factors to Consider**:
- **Patient Ability**: Physical, cognitive, and psychological capacity
- **Severity of Condition**: Potential for improvement may be limited in advanced disease
- **Treatment Efficacy**: What can existing treatments realistically achieve
- **Resources**: Access to care, medications, equipment, and support
- **Time Available**: Sufficient time to reach the goal
- **Motivation**: Patient's readiness to change and engagement

**Setting Achievable Goals**:
- Start with a baseline assessment
- Understand expected treatment effects (e.g., Metformin lowers HbA1c by 1-1.5%)
- Set incremental goals for major changes (lose 5 lbs first, then 10, rather than jumping to 50)
- Be challenging but not overwhelming
- Adjust goals based on progress

**Examples**:

| Unachievable | Achievable |
|----------------|------------|
| "Ready to run a marathon in 1 month" (Sedentary 70yo post-MI patient) | "Walk 1 mile continuously within 3 months" |
| "Lower HbA1c from 12% to <6% in 6 weeks" | "Lower HbA1c from 12% to <9% in 3 months, and <7% in 6 months" |
| "Achieve 0-140° knee ROM by post-op day 3" (Post-TKA) | "Achieve 0-90° knee ROM by week 2 and 0-110° by week 6" |
| "Cure chronic pain" | "Reduce pain from 7/10 to 4/10 and increase function by 30%" |

### 4. Relevant

Goals must align with the patient's values, priorities, and overall treatment objectives.

**Relevance Criteria**:
- **Patient-Centered**: Important to the patient; reflects their priorities
- **Clinically Significant**: Achieving the goal improves health or quality of life
- **Consistent with Diagnosis**: Targets the condition being treated
- **Appropriately Timed**: Right goal for the current stage of treatment
- **Integrated**: Fits with other treatment goals

**Assessing Relevance**:
- Ask the patient: "What matters most to you?" "What do you want to be able to do?"
- Ensure the goal addresses functional limitations the patient values
- Link clinical metrics to meaningful outcomes (e.g., "HbA1c <7% to reduce risk of vision loss")
- Avoid provider-driven goals that do not resonate with the patient

**Examples**:

| Low Relevance | High Relevance |
|---------------|---------------|
| "Reduce number of medications" (When meds control symptoms well) | "Simplify regimen to improve adherence" (If doses are missed due to complexity) |
| "Perfect blood sugar" (Patient's priority is energy) | "Improve energy levels through better glucose control" |
| "Walk 5 miles" (Patient just wants to shop independently) | "Navigate the grocery store without assistance" |

### 5. Time-Bound

Goals must have a specific deadline or timeframe for achievement.

**Timeline Considerations**:
- **Short-term goals**: Days to 3 months
- **Medium-term goals**: 3-6 months
- **Long-term goals**: 6-12 months or longer for chronic conditions
- **Re-evaluation intervals**: Checking progress at defined intervals

**Time Elements to Include**:
- Target date or timeframe
- Checkpoint dates for progress review
- Frequency of action (e.g., "Exercise 30 minutes, 5x per week")

**Examples**:

| Not Time-Bound | Time-Bound |
|----------------|------------|
| "Lose weight eventually" | "Lose 15 lbs within 6 months (approx. 1-2 lbs per week)" |
| "Attend physical therapy" | "Complete 12 PT sessions over 8 weeks, 1-2 times per week" |
| "Return to work when ready" | "Return to light-duty work within 12 weeks post-surgery" |
| "Improve depressive symptoms" | "Reduce PHQ-9 score from 18 to <10 within 8 weeks of starting SSRI and CBT" |

## Creating SMART Goals: A Step-by-Step Process

### Step 1: Assess Baseline
- Identify current status: symptoms, lab values, functional levels
- Use standardized assessments whenever possible
- Document the quantitative baseline

### Step 2: Identify Desired Outcomes
- What needs to improve?
- Engage the patient: "What would you like to be different?"
- Consider clinical requirements and patient priorities

### Step 3: Make it Specific
- Define the exact outcome
- Eliminate vague language
- Include all relevant details

### Step 4: Add Measurement
- How will progress be tracked?
- What metric or observable behavior will be used?
- Baseline → Target value

### Step 5: Reality Check (Is it Achievable?)
- Is this possible given the patient's condition, resources, and treatment efficacy?
- Adjust expectations if necessary
- Set incremental goals if needed

### Step 6: Ensure Relevance
- Does the patient care about this goal?
- Is it clinically meaningful?
- Does it align with the overall treatment plan?

### Step 7: Set a Timeline
- When should the goal be reached?
- When will progress be reviewed?
- Break into short-term and long-term if necessary

### Step 8: Document and Communicate
- Write the goal in clear SMART format
- Share with the patient and care team
- Ensure patient understanding

## Goal Hierarchies and Levels

### ICF Framework (International Classification of Functioning, Disability and Health)

Useful for rehabilitation and functional goals:

1. **Impairment-Level Goals**: Body structure/function
   - Example: "Increase shoulder flexion ROM from 90° to 140°"
   
2. **Activity-Level Goals**: Task performance
   - Example: "Independently perform upper body dressing"
   
3. **Participation-Level Goals**: Involvement in life roles
   - Example: "Return to work as a teacher"

### Medical Outcome Levels

1. **Biological/Clinical Goals**: Lab values, vitals, disease markers
   - Example: "HbA1c <7%, BP <130/80, LDL <70 mg/dL"
   
2. **Symptom Goals**: Patient-reported symptoms
   - Example: "Pain ≤4/10, no dyspnea during ADLs"
   
3. **Functional Goals**: What the patient can do
   - Example: "Walk 1 mile, climb two flights of stairs"
   
4. **Quality of Life Goals**: Overall well-being
   - Example: "Return to hobbies, improve sleep quality"

## Patient-Centered Outcome Measures (PCOMs)

### Definition
Outcomes that matter most to patients, in addition to traditional clinical metrics.

### Common PCOMs

**Patient-Reported Outcome Measures (PROMs)**:
- SF-36 or SF-12 (General health-related quality of life)
- PROMIS (Patient-Reported Outcomes Measurement Information System)
- Disease-specific QoL scales (e.g., Kansas City Cardiomyopathy Questionnaire for heart failure)

**Functional Outcomes**:
- Activities of Daily Living (ADLs): Bathing, dressing, toileting, transferring, eating, continence
- Instrumental ADLs (IADLs): Shopping, cooking, housekeeping, finances, transportation
- Occupational/Educational functioning
- Social functioning and relationships
- Participation in recreation and leisure

**Patient Priorities**:
- What matters most to the individual patient
- May differ from clinician priorities
- Examples: "Playing with grandchildren," "Attending a daughter's wedding," "Avoiding a nursing home"

### Integrating PCOMs into Goals

**Method**:
1. Ask about priorities early in the assessment
2. Link clinical goals to meaningful patient outcomes
3. Include at least some goals that directly target patient priorities
4. Use the patient's own language in goal documentation where possible

**Integration Example**:
- **Clinical Goal**: "Reduce HbA1c from 8.5% to <7% in 3 months"
- **Linked Patient-Centered Goal**: "Improve energy levels to play with grandchildren without fatigue"
- Document both and track progress for both

## Shared Decision-Making in Goal Setting

### What is Shared Decision-Making (SDM)?

A collaborative process where clinicians and patients work together to:
- Discuss treatment options
- Weigh risks and benefits
- Consider patient values and preferences
- Reach a mutual decision

### SDM in Treatment Goal Setting

**Steps**:

1. **Choice Awareness**: Acknowledge that multiple goals/approaches are possible
   - "We could focus on aggressive HbA1c reduction vs. minimizing hypoglycemia risk. Which is more important to you?"

2. **Option Presentation**: Present the pros/cons of goal scenarios
   - "Option A: Intensive BP control (<120/80) reduces stroke risk but requires more meds. Option B: Standard control (<140/90) is easier but carries slightly higher stroke risk."

3. **Value Clarification**: Understand the patient's priorities
   - "How do you feel about taking multiple medications?" "How important is avoiding injections to you?"

4. **Preference Integration**: Incorporate preferences into the goal
   - If patient prioritizes avoiding meds → "Control BP with lifestyle changes and one medication if possible"

5. **Decision**: Agree on the goal together
   - "It sounds like you want to try 3 months of intensive lifestyle changes before adding another medication. Let's set that as our plan."

6. **Documentation**: Record the shared decision process
   - "Goal established via shared decision-making. Patient expressed preference for..."

### Decision Aids

Tools to facilitate SDM:
- Option grids comparing different approaches
- Numerical data for risks/benefits
- Patient stories/testimonials
- Visual aids (pictures, graphs)
- "What Matters to You" worksheets

## Special Considerations for Different Populations

### Older Adults
- Functional independence often takes priority over disease-specific metrics
- Balance aggressive treatment with treatment burden
- Consider life expectancy and time-to-benefit
- Fall prevention and reducing polypharmacy may be key goals
- Quality of life over quantity of life

### Pediatrics
- Developmentally appropriate goals
- Family-centered (involving parents/caregivers)
- Growth and developmental milestones
- School/social functioning
- Transition planning (pediatric to adult care)

### Chronic Conditions
- Long-term sustainable goals
- Balance ambition with realistic expectations
- Prevention of complications
- Maintenance of quality of life
- Learning to adapt and cope alongside improvement

### Palliative/Hospice Care
- Comfort and symptom management as primary goals
- Functional goals focused on valued activities
- Psychosocial and spiritual needs
- Caregiver support
- Dignity and autonomy

### Multimorbidity (Complex Patients)
- Prioritize goals that have the most impact
- Coordinate goals across conditions (synergy)
- Avoid conflicting treatments
- Minimize treatment burden
- Realistic expectations for multiple conditions

## Common Goal Setting Pitfalls

### Pitfall 1: Clinician-Centered Goals
**Problem**: Goals reflect what the doctor thinks is important, not the patient's priorities  
**Fix**: Ask the patient what they hope to achieve early in the visit and use their language

### Pitfall 2: Too Many Goals
**Problem**: 10+ goals overwhelm the patient  
**Fix**: Prioritize 3-5 key goals and build from there

### Pitfall 3: All-or-Nothing Thinking
**Problem**: Goal is "to be cured" or "perfect"  
**Fix**: Set incremental goals and value meaningful improvement

### Pitfall 4: Ignoring Barriers
**Problem**: Setting goals without assessing feasibility (resources, support, access)  
**Fix**: Identify barriers during assessment and address them or adjust the goal

### Pitfall 5: Static Goals
**Problem**: Goals are set and never revisited  
**Fix**: Re-evaluate regularly and modify based on patient progress or changing circumstances

### Pitfall 6: Purely Clinical Metrics
**Problem**: All goals are lab values with no functional or QoL goals  
**Fix**: Balance clinical metrics with functional, symptom, and QoL outcomes

### Pitfall 7: Lack of Patient Buy-In
**Problem**: Patient doesn't see the goal as achievable or important  
**Fix**: Use shared decision-making and explore ambivalence via motivational interviewing

## Examples of SMART Goals by Condition

### Diabetes
**Short-term**: "Reduce HbA1c from 8.5% to <7.5% in 3 months by starting Metformin 1000mg BID and reducing carbohydrate intake to 45-60g per meal."

**Long-term**: "Maintain HbA1c <7% for >6 months to prevent microvascular complications and improve energy levels for 30 minutes of daily walking."

### Heart Failure
**Short-term**: "Achieve euvolemia (no edema, weight stable within 2 lbs) within 2 weeks by optimizing Furosemide dosing and limiting sodium intake to <2000mg/day."

**Long-term**: "Maintain NYHA Class II functional status, prevent HF hospitalizations, and achieve walking 1/4 mile without dyspnea within 3 months."

### Depression
**Short-term**: "Reduce PHQ-9 score from 18 to <10 within 8 weeks by starting Escitalopram 10mg daily and attending weekly CBT sessions."

**Long-term**: "Achieve remission of depression (PHQ-9 <5) within 4 months, return to full-time employment, and re-engage in social activities with friends 2-3 times per month."

### Post-Stroke Rehabilitation
**Short-term**: "Increase right arm strength from 2/5 to 3+/5 and improve Functional Independence Measure (FIM) score from 85 to 100 within 4 weeks of PT/OT 5x/week."

**Long-term**: "Achieve independence in all ADLs, ambulate 500 feet with a cane on level surfaces, and return to home environment (vs. skilled nursing) within 3 months."

### Chronic Low Back Pain
**Short-term**: "Reduce pain intensity from 7/10 to 4/10 and increase walking tolerance from 10 to 30 minutes within 6 weeks using multimodal analgesia (SNRI, NSAID, PT)."

**Long-term**: "Return to light-duty work within 3 months, participate in personal hobbies (fishing, modified gardening), and reduce pain interference with daily life by 50% (Brief Pain Inventory)."

### Hypertension
**Short-term**: "Reduce BP from 152/94 to <140/90 mmHg within 4 weeks by starting Lisinopril 10mg daily and reducing sodium intake to <2300mg/day."

**Long-term**: "Achieve and maintain BP <130/80 mmHg to reduce 10-year ASCVD risk from 15% to <10% and prevent cardiovascular events."
