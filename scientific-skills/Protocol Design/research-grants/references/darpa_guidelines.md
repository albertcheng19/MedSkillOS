# DARPA (Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency) Proposal Writing Guide

## Agency Overview

**Mission**: To make pivotal investments in breakthrough technologies for national security.

**Slogan**: "Creating Breakthrough Technologies and Capabilities for National Security"

**Annual Budget**: Approximately $4 billion

**Website**: https://www.darpa.mil

**Core Characteristics**:
- High-risk, high-reward research
- Focus on revolutionary breakthroughs rather than incremental improvements
- Technology transition to military and commercial applications
- Program Managers (PMs) possess extensive autonomy
- Programs with clear end-goals and cycles of approximately 3-5 years
- Emphasis on prototyping and demonstrations
- Tackling "DARPA-hard" problems that others are unwilling or unable to solve

**What Makes DARPA Unique**:
- Not basic research (that is the role of ONR, AFOSR, ARO)
- Not development and procurement (that is the task of Service acquisition branches)
- Focused on the bridge from proof-of-concept to the prototype stage
- Tolerates and anticipates failure in the pursuit of breakthroughs
- Rapid transition to operational use

## DARPA Organizational Structure

### Six Technical Offices

#### 1. BTO (Biological Technologies Office)
**Focus**: Leveraging biology as technology, human-machine interaction, synthetic biology.

**Example Programs**:
- Neural interfaces and brain-computer interfaces
- Synthetic biology and Living Foundries
- Pandemic prevention and response
- Human performance enhancement
- Biomanufacturing technologies

#### 2. DSO (Defense Sciences Office)
**Focus**: High-risk, high-reward research in physical and mathematical sciences.

**Example Programs**:
- Novel materials and chemistry
- Quantum technologies
- Electromagnetics and photonics
- Mathematics and algorithms
- Fundamental limits of physics

#### 3. I2O (Information Innovation Office)
**Focus**: Gaining information advantage through computing, communications, and cyberspace.

**Example Programs**:
- Artificial Intelligence and Machine Learning
- Cybersecurity and cyber resilience
- Communications and networking
- Data analytics and processing
- Human-computer interaction

#### 4. MTO (Microsystems Technology Office)
**Focus**: Microelectronics, photonics, and heterogeneous microsystems.

**Example Programs**:
- Advanced electronics and integrated circuits
- Photonics and optical systems
- Novel computing architectures
- RF and millimeter-wave systems
- MEMS (Micro-Electro-Mechanical Systems) and sensors

#### 5. STO (Strategic Technology Office)
**Focus**: Technologies for space, air, maritime, and ground systems.

**Example Programs**:
- Autonomous systems (Air, Ground, Sea, Space)
- Advanced propulsion and energy
- Space technologies
- Electronic warfare
- Long-range precision fires

#### 6. TTO (Tactical Technology Office)
**Focus**: Near-term technologies for ground, maritime, and expeditionary forces.

**Example Programs**:
- Tactical autonomy
- Advanced weaponry
- Urban operations
- Maneuver and logistics
- Special operations support

## How DARPA Operates

### The Program Manager-Centric Model

**Program Managers (PMs)**:
- Approximately 100 PMs across DARPA
- Recruited from academia, industry, and government labs for 3-5 year rotations
- Possess high autonomy in creating and running programs
- Identify "DARPA-hard" problems and seek solutions
- Manage portfolios consisting of 10-20 projects

**Program Manager Lifecycle**:
1. **Build Vision**: Identify transformative opportunities
2. **Create Program**: Design research thrusts and metrics
3. **Issue BAA**: Release "Broad Agency Announcements" to solicit proposals
4. **Select Team**: Choose performers and build the program structure
5. **Manage Program**: Track milestones, pivot direction, and transition technology
6. **Transition**: Hand off successful technology to Services or industry

**Implications for Proposers**:
- The PM owns the vision—your job is to execute it
- Contact the PM before submitting a proposal (almost mandatory)
- Understand the PM's technical vision and goals
- Build a relationship with the PM (within ethical guidelines)

### The "DARPA-hard" Test

**Three questions every DARPA project must answer**:

1. **What are you trying to do?**
   - Articulate your objectives using absolutely no jargon
   - Clear, specific technical goals

2. **How is it done today, and what are the limits of current practice?**
   - What is the State of the Art (SOTA)?
   - Why are current approaches insufficient?
   - What fundamental barriers exist?

3. **What is new in your approach and why do you think it will be successful?**
   - What is the breakthrough insight or capability?
   - Why hasn't this been done before?
   - What has changed now to make it possible?

**Other Considerations**:
- **Who cares?** (What is the impact on national security?)
- **What if you are right?** (What will become possible?)
- **What if you are wrong?** (Is the risk manageable?)
- **What if you succeed?** (Is there a transition path?)

**DARPA Seeks**:
- **High Risk**: A 50% failure rate is acceptable
- **High Reward**: 10x improvements, not 10% improvements
- **Measurable**: Clear success metrics
- **Transitionable**: A path to operational use or commercial adoption

## DARPA Solicitation Types

### 1. Broad Agency Announcements (BAAs)

**Most Common Mechanism**: Open solicitations for specific program areas.

**Characteristics**:
- Issued by PMs for specific programs
- Describe technical goals and research thrusts
- Feature multiple submission deadlines or rolling submissions
- Formal proposals are typically 20-40 pages
- Usually require an initial Abstract or White Paper

**Types of BAAs**:

**Program BAAs**: Targeted at a specific named program
- Clear technical goals and metrics
- Defined research areas (thrusts)
- Specified deliverables and milestones
- PM is known and the vision is clear

**Office-Wide BAAs**: General solicitations issued by technical offices
- Broader scope, less prescriptive
- Seeking transformative ideas
- More flexibility in approach
- May contain multiple areas of interest

### 2. Small Business Innovation Research (SBIR)

**Targeted at Small Businesses**:
- **Phase I**: $150k-$250k, 6-9 months (Feasibility study)
- **Phase II**: $1M-$2M, 2 years (Development phase)
- **Phase III**: Non-SBIR funding (Commercialization)

### 3. Proposers Days and Special Notices

**Proposers Days**: Pre-solicitation events
- PM presents the program vision and goals
- Q&A interaction with potential proposers
- Networking opportunities for team building
- Attendance is usually required or strongly encouraged

**Special Notices**: Requests for Information (RFIs), teaming opportunities

## DARPA Proposal Structure

**Note**: Formats vary by BAA. **Always strictly follow the specific BAA instructions.**

### Typical Structure

#### Volume I: Technical and Management Proposal (20-40 pages)

**Section 1: Executive Summary** (1-2 pages)
- Overview of proposed research
- Technical approach and innovations
- Expected outcomes and deliverables
- Team qualifications
- Alignment with BAA goals

**Section 2: Goals and Impact** (2-3 pages)
- Problem statement
- Significance and national security relevance
- State of the art and limitations
- How your work will advance the state of the art
- Impact if successful (What if it's true? Who cares?)
- Alignment with DARPA program goals

**Section 3: Technical Approach and Innovation** (10-20 pages)
- Detailed technical plan organized by phase or thrust
- Novel approach and why it will succeed
- Technical risks and mitigation strategies
- Preliminary results or proof-of-concept data
- Technical barriers and how to overcome them
- Innovation and differentiation from existing work

**Organization by Phase** (Typical):

**Phase I (Feasibility)**: 12-18 months
- Technical goals and milestones
- Approach and methodology
- Expected outcomes
- Success metrics
- Go/no-go criteria for Phase II

**Phase II (Development)**: 18-24 months
- Building on Phase I results
- System integration and optimization
- Testing and validation
- Prototype development
- Metrics and evaluation

**Phase III (Demonstration)**: 12-18 months (if applicable)
- Field testing or operational demonstration
- Transition activities
- Hand-off to transition partners

**Section 4: Capabilities and Resources** (2-3 pages)
- Team qualifications and expertise
- Facilities and equipment
- Relevant prior work and publications
- Roles of subcontractors and collaborators
- Organizational structure

**Section 5: Statement of Work (SOW)** (3-5 pages)
- Detailed task breakdown
- Deliverables for each task
- Milestones and metrics
- Timeline (Gantt chart)
- Dependencies and critical path
- Government Furnished Property or Information (if applicable)

**Section 6: Schedule and Milestones** (1-2 pages)
- Integrated master schedule
- Key decision points
- Deliverable timeline
- Go/no-go criteria
- Reporting and meeting schedule

**Section 7: Technology Transition Plan** (2-3 pages)
- Potential transition partners (Services, industry)
- Path to operational use or commercialization
- Market or operational analysis
- Transition activities during the program
- Intellectual Property (IP) and licensing strategy (if applicable)

#### Volume II: Cost Proposal (Separate Volume)

**Detailed Budget**:
- Costs by phase, task, and year
- Labor (personnel, hours, rates)
- Materials and supplies
- Equipment
- Travel
- Subcontracts
- Other Direct Costs (ODCs)
- Indirect costs (Overhead, G&A)
- Fee or profit (for industry)

**Cost Justification**:
- Rationale for cost elements
- Labor categories and rates
- Basis of estimate
- Cost realism analysis
- Supporting documentation

**Supporting Documents**:
- Cost Accounting Standards (CAS)
- Approved indirect rate agreements
- Subcontractor quotes or cost proposals

#### Other Volumes (if required)

**Appendices**:
- Quad charts (1-page slide summary)
- Relevant publications or technical papers
- Letters of commitment from collaborators
- Facilities descriptions
- Equipment lists

## Evaluation Criteria

### DARPA Evaluation Factors (Typical)

**Primary Criteria** (usually weighted equally):

1. **Overall Scientific and Technical Merit**
   - Technical soundness and feasibility
   - Innovation and novelty
   - Likelihood of achieving goals
   - Technical approach and methodology
   - Understanding of the problem and SOTA
   - Risk and risk mitigation

2. **Potential Contribution and Relevance to DARPA Mission**
   - Alignment with program goals
   - National security impact
   - Advancement of the state of the art
   - Potential for revolutionary breakthrough
   - The "What if it's true? Who cares?" test

3. **Cost Realism and Reasonableness**
   - Budget tied to technical plan
   - Costs are reasonable and realistic
   - Value for investment
   - Cost-benefit analysis

4. **Capabilities and Related Experience**
   - Team qualifications and track record
   - Adequacy of facilities and resources
   - Relevant prior work
   - Ability to deliver on time and on budget
   - Management approach

5. **Technology Transition**
   - Path to operational use or market
   - Transition partnerships
   - Market analysis (if applicable)
   - Follow-on development plan
   - IP strategy supporting transition
