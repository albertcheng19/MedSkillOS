# Evidence-Based Intervention Guidelines

## Overview

This reference guide is designed to provide comprehensive guidance on selecting, implementing, and documenting evidence-based interventions covering pharmacological, non-pharmacological, and surgical treatment modalities. These guidelines support the development of treatment plans through current best practices and clinical recommendations.

## Evidence Grading

### Levels of Evidence

**Level I: Highest Quality**
- Systematic reviews and meta-analyses of randomized controlled trials (RCTs)
- Large multi-center randomized controlled trials

**Level II: High Quality**
- Single randomized controlled trial
- Systematic reviews of observational studies

**Level III: Moderate Quality**
- Cohort studies
- Case-control studies
- Well-designed observational studies

**Level IV: Lower Quality**
- Case series
- Case reports
- Expert opinion

**Strength of Recommendation**:
- **Grade A**: Strong recommendation, high-quality evidence
- **Grade B**: Moderate recommendation, moderate-quality evidence
- **Grade C**: Weak recommendation, low-quality evidence
- **Grade D**: Not recommended (evidence of harm or no benefit)

## Pharmacological Interventions

### Principles of Medication Selection

#### 1. Evidence-Based Prescribing
- Use medications proven effective for the indication
- Follow clinical practice guidelines
- Consider comparative effectiveness research data
- Prioritize medications with higher safety profiles when efficacy is comparable

#### 2. Patient-Specific Factors
- Comorbidities and contraindications
- Organ function (renal, hepatic)
- Drug allergies and intolerances
- Concomitant medications (drug-drug interactions)
- Age, pregnancy status
- Genetic factors (refer to pharmacogenomics if available)
- Cost and insurance coverage

#### 3. Medication Safety
- Start low, go slow (especially for elderly or patients with multiple comorbidities)
- Titrate to target dose based on response and tolerance
- Monitor for adverse reactions
- Avoid potentially inappropriate medications (refer to Beers Criteria for the elderly)
- Minimize polypharmacy whenever possible

### Common Medication Classes by Indication

#### Hypertension

**First-line agents** (based on JNC-8, ACC/AHA guidelines):
- **ACE inhibitors** (lisinopril, enalapril): Preferred in patients with diabetes, CKD, or heart failure
- **ARBs** (losartan, valsartan): Alternative for patients unable to tolerate ACEIs
- **Calcium Channel Blockers (CCB)** (amlodipine): Particularly effective in elderly and Black patients
- **Thiazide diuretics** (chlorthalidone, HCTZ): Cost-effective with good cardiovascular outcomes

**Dosing strategies**:
- Start with a low dose of a single agent
- Titrate to maximum tolerated dose before adding a second agent
- Combination therapy (2-3 agents) is often required
- Monitor blood pressure response and adjust every 2-4 weeks

#### Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus

**First-line agents** (ADA Standards of Care):
- **Metformin**: First-line for all patients unless contraindicated (eGFR <30)
  - Starting dose 500-850mg once or twice daily, titrate to a total daily dose of 2000mg

**Second-line agents** (individualized selection based on comorbidities):
- **SGLT2 inhibitors** (empagliflozin, dapagliflozin): For patients with heart failure or CKD (significant cardiorenal benefits)
- **GLP-1 receptor agonists** (semaglutide, dulaglutide): For patients with ASCVD or high risk, or when weight loss is needed
- **DPP-4 inhibitors** (sitagliptin): When very low hypoglycemia risk is required
- **Sulfonylureas** (glipizide): Cost-effective but carries hypoglycemia risk
- **Insulin**: If HbA1c is significantly elevated ($>$10%) or symptoms of hyperglycemia are present

#### Depression

**First-line SSRIs** (APA guidelines):
- Sertraline, escitalopram, fluoxetine, citalopram, paroxetine
- Start at low doses (e.g., sertraline 50mg, escitalopram 10mg)
- Titrate after 2-4 weeks if there is a partial response
- Full trial: 6-8 weeks at therapeutic dose
- Continue for 6-12 months after remission (longer if recurrent)

**Second-line agents**:
- **SNRIs** (venlafaxine, duloxetine): Especially if comorbid chronic pain is present
- **Bupropion**: If sexual dysfunction is a concern or smoking cessation is needed
- **Mirtazapine**: If improvement in insomnia/appetite stimulation is needed

**Augmentation therapy** (if response is incomplete):
- Combine with an antidepressant from another class
- Atypical antipsychotics (aripiprazole, quetiapine) - FDA-approved augmentation
- Lithium, thyroid hormone (triiodothyronine)

#### Chronic Pain

**Multimodal analgesia** (WHO Pain Ladder, CDC Opioid Guidelines):

**Non-opioid analgesics**:
- **Acetaminophen**: 3-4g daily in divided doses, safe with normal liver function
- **NSAIDs**: Ibuprofen, naproxen, meloxicam - short-term use or long-term with GI protection
  - Monitor: Renal function, blood pressure, GI bleed risk

**Adjuvant analgesics for neuropathic pain**:
- **Gabapentin**: Start at 300mg, titrate to 1800-3600mg daily in three divided doses
- **Pregabalin**: 75mg BID, titrate to 150-300mg BID (better bioavailability than gabapentin)
- **SNRIs** (duloxetine): 60mg daily for diabetic neuropathy, chronic musculoskeletal pain
- **TCAs** (amitriptyline, nortriptyline): Low dose (10-75mg QHS) - second-line due to side effects

**Topical agents**:
- Lidocaine patch 5%, diclofenac gel, capsaicin cream
- Local action with minimal systemic absorption

**Opioids** (CDC Guidelines - use with caution):
- Use only when non-opioid multimodal therapy is insufficient
- Start with the lowest effective dose, short-acting formulations preferred
- Avoid $>$90 MME/day whenever possible
- Requires monitoring with UDS (urine drug screen), PDMP (prescription drug monitoring program), and co-prescribing naloxone
- Frequent assessment; taper if functional goals are not met

#### Heart Failure with Reduced Ejection Fraction (HFrEF)

**Guideline-Directed Medical Therapy (GDMT)** - The "Four Pillars":

1. **ACE Inhibitor or ARB or ARNI**
   - ACE: Lisinopril 20-40mg daily, enalapril 10-20mg BID
   - ARNI (Sacubitril/Valsartan): Start 24/26mg BID → Target 97/103mg BID (superior to ACE/ARB)
   - Monitor: BP, renal function, potassium

2. **Beta-blockers**
   - Carvedilol 3.125-6.25mg BID → 25mg BID (target)
   - Metoprolol succinate 12.5-25mg daily → 200mg daily
   - Bisoprolol 1.25mg → 10mg daily
   - Titrate slowly, monitor heart rate and BP

3. **Mineralocorticoid Receptor Antagonists (MRA)**
   - Spironolactone 12.5-25mg daily (max 50mg)
   - Eplerenone 25mg daily → 50mg daily
   - Monitor: Potassium, renal function (risk of hyperkalemia)

4. **SGLT2 Inhibitors**
   - Dapagliflozin 10mg daily or empagliflozin 10mg daily
   - Reduces heart failure hospitalizations and mortality
   - Beneficial for diabetes and CKD as well

**Other therapies**:
- Loop diuretics (furosemide) for volume management (no mortality benefit)
- Hydralazine-Isosorbide Dinitrate (for African American patients or those intolerant to ACE/ARB)
- Ivabradine (if EF $\leq$35%, HR $>$70 on max beta-blocker)
- Digoxin (symptom improvement, reduced hospitalization)

### Best Practices for Medication Documentation

**The treatment plan should include**:
- Generic name (brand name optional)
- Dose, route, frequency
- Indication/Rationale
- Titration schedule (if applicable)
- Expected timeline for benefit
- Key side effects to monitor
- Drug interactions
- Criteria for adjustment or discontinuation

**Example**: "Lisinopril 10mg PO daily - ACE inhibitor for hypertension and diabetic renal protection. Titrate to 20mg in 2-4 weeks if BP not at goal and well-tolerated (monitor for cough, hyperkalemia). Target BP <130/80."

## Non-Pharmacological Interventions

### Lifestyle Interventions

#### Diet and Nutrition

**Mediterranean Diet** (Evidence: Multiple RCTs, PREDIMED trial):
- **Indications**: Cardiovascular disease prevention, diabetes management
- **Components**:
  - High intake: Fruits, vegetables, whole grains, legumes, nuts, olive oil
  - Moderate: Fish, poultry
  - Low intake: Red meat, sweets
- **Evidence**: 30% reduction in cardiovascular events, improved glycemic control
- **Implementation**: Referral to a dietitian for Medical Nutrition Therapy (MNT)

**DASH Diet** (Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension):
- **Indications**: Hypertension
- **Components**: High fruit/vegetable, low-fat dairy, reduced sodium intake (<2300mg, ideally <1500mg)
- **Evidence**: Reduces systolic BP by 8-14 mmHg
- **Implementation**: DASH diet plan education, sodium intake tracking

**Carbohydrate Counting** (for Diabetes):
- Consistent carbohydrate intake: 45-60g per meal
- Facilitates insulin dose adjustment
- Prevents glycemic excursions
- Taught by a dietitian for carb-counting skills

**Weight Management**:
- Caloric deficit: Reduce 500-750 kcal daily to achieve 1-2 lbs weight loss per week
- Behavioral change strategies: Self-monitoring, stimulus control, goal setting
- Structured programs (e.g., Weight Watchers, MOVE!) are more effective than self-management
- For BMI $\geq$30-35 with comorbidities, consider medications (GLP-1 agonists, orlistat) or bariatric surgery

#### Physical Activity and Exercise

**Aerobic Exercise**:
- **Recommendation**: 150 minutes of moderate-intensity OR 75 minutes of vigorous-intensity exercise per week
- **Moderate intensity**: Brisk walking, cycling, swimming - can talk but not sing during exercise
- **Vigorous intensity**: Running, fast cycling - need to pause for breath while talking
- **Benefits**: Cardiovascular health, glycemic control, weight management, mood regulation
- **Implementation**: Start with 10 minutes at a time and gradually increase

**Resistance Training**:
- **Recommendation**: 2-3 times per week, covering all major muscle groups
- **Benefits**: Muscle strength, bone density, metabolic rate, glycemic control
- **Implementation**: Bodyweight exercises, resistance bands, free weights, machines

**Balance and Flexibility**:
- Crucial for fall prevention in the elderly
- Yoga, Tai Chi
- Stretching routines

**Exercise Prescription**:
- FITT Principle: **F**requency, **I**ntensity, **T**ime, **T**ype
- Individualized based on fitness level, comorbidities, and goals
- Cardiac evaluation if indicated (refer to ACSM or ACC/AHA guidelines)

**Example**: "Aerobic Exercise: Brisk walking 30 minutes, 5 days/week, moderate intensity (50-70% of max HR). Resistance Training: Full body muscle exercises 2 times/week, 2 sets of 10-12 reps."

#### Smoking Cessation

**Evidence**: Most powerful intervention for COPD, cardiovascular disease, and cancer prevention

**5 A's Approach**:
1. **Ask**: Screen all patients for tobacco use
2. **Advise**: Urge all tobacco users to quit
3. **Assess**: Determine willingness to quit
4. **Assist**: Aid in quitting (counseling + pharmacotherapy)
5. **Arrange**: Schedule follow-up

**Pharmacotherapy** (can double quit rates):
- **Nicotine Replacement Therapy (NRT)**: Patches, gum, lozenges - OTC, safe
- **Varenicline**: Most effective (Chantix), start 1 week before quit date
- **Bupropion**: Alternative, also treats depression
- **Combination therapy**: NRT + Varenicline/Bupropion is more effective

**Counseling**:
- Quitlines: 1-800-QUIT-NOW
- Individual or group counseling
- Cognitive-behavioral techniques

**Implementation**: Set a quit date within 30 days, prescribe medications and refer for counseling, follow up within 1 week after the quit date.

#### Sleep Hygiene

**Indications**: Insomnia, poor sleep quality

**Components**:
- Regular sleep-wake cycle (fixed bedtime/wake time)
- Bedroom environment: Dark, quiet, cool (60-67°F)
- Avoid: Caffeine after 2 PM, alcohol, large meals before bed
- Screen time: Stop 1 hour before bed
- Relaxation routine: Reading, bathing, relaxation exercises
- Bed is for sleep only (no TV, no work)
- If not asleep after 20 minutes, get up and do a quiet activity

**Evidence**: Effective for chronic insomnia, often combined with Cognitive Behavioral Therapy for Insomnia (CBT-I)

#### Stress Management

**Techniques**:
- **Mindfulness Meditation**: 10-20 minutes daily, reduces anxiety and depression
- **Progressive Muscle Relaxation**: Systematically tensing and relaxing muscle groups
- **Deep Breathing**: Diaphragmatic breathing, 4-7-8 technique
- **Yoga, Tai Chi**: Mind-body practices
- **Cognitive Reframing**: Challenging stress-inducing thoughts

**Evidence**: Lowers stress hormones, improves mood and pain perception

### Behavioral Interventions

#### Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)

**Indications**: Depression, anxiety, insomnia, chronic pain, substance abuse

**Core Components**:
- Psychoeducation
- Cognitive reframing (identifying and challenging distorted thoughts)
- Behavioral activation (increasing rewarding activities)
- Problem-solving skills
- Relapse prevention

**Evidence**: Efficacy for mild-to-moderate depression is equivalent to antidepressants; first-line for anxiety and insomnia

**Implementation**: 12-16 weekly 50-minute sessions with a trained therapist, including homework assignments

**Variants**:
- **CBT-I** (Insomnia): Sleep restriction, stimulus control, cognitive therapy for sleep
- **CBT-CP** (Chronic Pain): Pain education, activity pacing, cognitive reframing of pain catastrophizing

#### Motivational Interviewing (MI)

**Indications**: Ambivalence toward behavior change (diet, exercise, substance abuse, medication adherence)

**Principles**:
- Express empathy
- Develop discrepancy (between current behavior and goals/values)
- Roll with resistance (avoid arguing)
- Support self-efficacy

**Techniques**:
- Open-ended questions
- Affirmations
- Reflective listening
- Summarizing
- Eliciting "change talk"

**Evidence**: Highly effective in initiating behavior change across multiple domains

### Patient Education and Self-Management

**Components**:
- Disease education (pathophysiology, natural history, treatment)
- Self-monitoring skills (glucose, BP, weight, symptoms)
- Medication management (purpose, dose, side effects)
- Symptom recognition and action plans
- Lifestyle change skills
- Problem-solving abilities
- When to seek medical attention

**Evidence**: Self-management education improves outcomes in diabetes, asthma, heart failure, and chronic pain

**Delivery**:
- Individual education by clinicians or educators
- Structured programs (e.g., DSMES for diabetes, cardiac rehab)
- Group classes
- Written materials, videos, apps

## Procedures and Interventional Therapies

### Rehabilitation Therapy

#### Physical Therapy (PT)

**Indications**: Musculoskeletal injuries, post-operative recovery, balance/gait disorders, chronic pain

**Interventions**:
- Therapeutic exercise: Strength, stretching, endurance training
- Manual therapy: Soft tissue mobilization, joint mobilization
- Gait and balance training
- Physical modalities: Heat, ice, ultrasound, electrical stimulation, TENS
- Functional training: ADL (Activities of Daily Living) retraining, body mechanics education

**Evidence**: Strong evidence for specific conditions (e.g., PT for knee OA is equivalent to NSAIDs for pain and function)

**Prescription**: Frequency (e.g., 2-3 times/week), duration (e.g., 4-8 weeks), specific interventions/goals

#### Occupational Therapy (OT)

**Indications**: ADL limitations, upper extremity dysfunction, cognitive-perceptual deficits, work-related injuries

**Interventions**:
- ADL/IADL training
- Assistive devices and environmental modifications
- Upper extremity strength and coordination training
- Energy conservation techniques
- Cognitive rehabilitation
- Work hardening/adaptation

**Evidence**: Increases independence post-stroke, post-injury, and in chronic conditions

#### Speech-Language Pathology (SLP)

**Indications**: Dysphagia, aphasia, dysarthria, cognitive-communication disorders

**Interventions**:
- Swallowing therapy and diet modification
- Language therapy (aphasia)
- Articulation therapy
- Cognitive-linguistic therapy
- Augmentative and Alternative Communication (AAC)

### Interventional Pain Procedures

#### Epidural Steroid Injections (ESI)

**Indications**: Radicular pain from disc herniation or spinal stenosis

**Evidence**: Moderate-quality evidence for short-term (3-6 weeks to 3 months) pain relief; long-term benefits vary

**Approach**: Transforaminal, interlaminar, or caudal under fluoroscopic guidance

**Frequency**: Maximum 3-4 injections per year

**Risks**: Infection, bleeding, nerve injury (rare), dural puncture

#### Radiofrequency Ablation (RFA)

**Indications**: Facetogenic pain after positive diagnostic medial branch blocks

**Evidence**: Good evidence for lumbar facet pain, providing relief for 6-12 months

**Procedure**: Thermal lesioning of the medial branch nerves supplying the facet joints

**Repeatability**: Can be repeated if pain recurs

#### Spinal Cord Stimulation (SCS)

**Indications**: Refractory chronic neuropathic pain (Failed Back Surgery Syndrome, CRPS, diabetic neuropathy)

**Evidence**: 50-60% of patients achieve $\geq$50% pain relief and improved function

**Procedure**: Trial lead placement (5-7 days), followed by permanent implantation if successful

**Technologies**: Tonic, high-frequency, burst, and Dorsal Root Ganglion (DRG) stimulation

### Surgical Interventions

**When to refer for surgery**:
- Failure of conservative management (after adequate trial - usually at least 6-12 weeks)
- Progressive neurological deficits
- Cauda Equina Syndrome (emergency)
- Functional limitations severely impacting quality of life
- Structural pathology amenable to surgical correction
- Patient preference after discussion of risks/benefits

**Shared Decision Making**: Discuss surgical vs. non-surgical management, risks, benefits, expected outcomes, and recovery

## Integrative and Complementary Therapies

### Acupuncture

**Evidence**:
- **Moderate evidence**: For chronic low back pain, knee osteoarthritis pain, tension headaches, and migraines
- **Mechanism**: Not fully understood (endorphin release, gate control theory, placebo effect)

**Implementation**: 8-12 sessions with a licensed acupuncturist

### Massage Therapy

**Evidence**: Some benefit for chronic low back pain, anxiety, and cancer-related symptoms

**Types**: Swedish, deep tissue, myofascial release

**Implementation**: 30-60 minute sessions, 1-2 times per week

### Yoga

**Evidence**: Improves low back pain, balance, and flexibility; reduces stress and anxiety

**Types**: Hatha (gentle), Vinyasa (flowing), Iyengar (alignment-focused)

**Implementation**: Group classes or home practice, 2-3 times per week

### Mindfulness-Based Stress Reduction (MBSR)

**Evidence**: Reduces stress, anxiety, depression, and chronic pain

**Program**: 8-week structured program with 2.5-hour weekly sessions and daily home practice

**Components**: Meditation, body scanning, mindful movement (yoga)

### Chiropractic Care

**Evidence**: Effective for acute and chronic low back pain and neck pain

**Techniques**: Spinal manipulation, mobilization, soft tissue therapy

**Safety**: Generally safe, but high-velocity manipulation should be avoided in cases of osteoporosis or spinal instability

## Intervention Selection and Documentation

### Treatment Pathway Approach

1. **Diagnosis Specificity**: Follow evidence-based guidelines for the specific condition
2. **Severity Matching**: Mild → Conservative; Severe → Aggressive
3. **Stepped Escalation**: Start with first-line therapy, add or switch if response is inadequate
4. **Multimodal**: Combine complementary interventions (Pharmacological + Non-pharmacological)
5. **Individualization**: Adjust based on patient factors (comorbidities, preferences, resources)

### Documentation Template

For each intervention, document the following:
- **Intervention**: Specific name/type
- **Indication**: Why this intervention is being used for this patient
- **Evidence**: Guideline basis or RCT data supporting use
- **Dose/Frequency/Duration**: Specific parameters
- **Expected Benefit**: What will improve, by how much, and when
- **Monitoring**: How response will be assessed
- **Risks/Side Effects**: Key issues to monitor
- **Alternatives Considered**: What else was considered and why it was not chosen
